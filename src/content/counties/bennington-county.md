## Bennington County: Outlet Retail Gateway and Manufacturing Legacy

Bennington County—Vermont's southwest corner—stretches from the city of Bennington (population 8,700) through Manchester's luxury retail and outlet shopping district, to Arlington's historic village character, and into mountain towns. The county's Grand List exceeds $2.2 billion, with median property tax bills around $3,600–$4,800 annually.

Bennington County's economy is driven by contradictory forces that confuse property assessments:

- **Manchester outlet shopping dominates retail.** The Vermont Premium Outlets and designer outlets draw regional traffic. Yet this premium should not apply to non-outlet retail in other towns.
- **Bennington College and cultural institutions.** Bennington College, museums, galleries, and summer theater season drive some tourism and employment. Yet the college is shrinking; enrollment has declined.
- **Stratton and Magic Mountain ski areas.** Both are modest compared to Killington. Seasonal tourism is real but not dominant. Properties assessed on peak ski season assumptions overvalue year-round property.
- **Bennington manufacturing legacy (fading).** Bennington's industrial base (printing, die-casting, metal fabrication) has contracted. Commercial-industrial properties are overvalued on cost approach, not income approach.
- **Route 7 and New York border commuter appeal.** Some residential demand from NY commuters, but this is modest. Properties assessed on "gateway to Vermont" premium may be overstated.

**Our average client savings in Bennington County: $2,000–$14,000 annually.** Manchester luxury retail properties average $8,000–$14,000 reductions; Bennington and smaller town commercial properties average $5,000–$10,000; residential properties average $3,000–$7,000.

## Residential Overtaxation in Bennington County

### Manchester: Luxury Market Correction

Manchester is Vermont's most expensive residential market (excluding Stowe). Yet prices have corrected modestly from 2022 peak. Residential properties assessed on 2022–2023 valuations are overvalued by 8–15%.

A Manchester residential property (4-bedroom colonial, 3,000 SF, on 2 acres, assessed $625,000 in 2023) is overvalued. Current comparable sales (Feb–Mar 2026) show $540–$590K range. The property assessed at $625K is 6–15% high.

Manchester premium is real but has compressed. Assessments must adjust to current market reality.

### Bennington: Manufacturing Town Residential Softening

Bennington's residential market is moderate. Yet properties are assessed on optimistic economic assumptions. A Bennington residential property (3-bedroom colonial, 1,800 SF, assessed $235,000) in a neighborhood near closed manufacturing facilities should be assessed $200–$220K.

Manufacturing facility closures and downsizing have reduced residential demand. Properties in affected neighborhoods face 10–20% discounts. Assessments have not caught up.

### Arlington and Mountain Towns: Scenic Premium Inflation

Arlington and small mountain towns are assessed with "scenic premium" uplift that doesn't match market reality. A property with mountain views is valued 10–15% higher, even though actual buyer demand for the mountain view is modest.

An Arlington residential property (3-bedroom ranch, 1,600 SF, mountain views, assessed $280,000) should be assessed $245–$265K. The mountain view premium is real but overstated by Listers (typically 3–5%, not 10%+).

## Commercial Property Overtaxation in Bennington County

### Manchester: Outlet Premium Spillover

Manchester's commercial value is driven by outlet shopping and luxury retail. Yet Listers sometimes apply outlet-level rents ($20–$28/SF) and valuations to general retail that doesn't have outlet traffic.

A Manchester non-outlet retail building (2,000 SF, second-tier location, not on main outlet thoroughfare):
- Lister's assumption: $22/SF retail rent, 90% occupancy = $39,600 gross
- Actual retail comps: $14–$16/SF in secondary locations
- Actual occupancy: 70–75%
- Actual rent: 2,000 × 15/SF × 72% = $21,600 gross
- Operating expenses: $7,200
- Lister's assumed NOI: $32,400
- Actual NOI: $14,400
- Cap rate (outlet risk): 6.5%
- Assessed value: $499,000 (if 6.5% cap on $32.4K NOI)
- True value: $221,538 (if $14.4K NOI ÷ 6.5%)
- Overvaluation: $277,462 (56%)

### Bennington: Downtown Commercial Decline

Bennington's downtown has experienced slow commercial decline. Properties are assessed on assumptions of downtown vitality that don't match current occupancy.

A downtown Bennington office/retail building (4-story, 6,000 SF total):
- Lister's assumption: mixed-use at blended $13/SF with 85% occupancy = $66,300 gross
- Actual reality: occupancy 65%, effective rent $10/SF = $39,000 gross
- Operating expenses: $13,000
- Lister's assumed NOI: $53,300
- Actual NOI: $26,000
- Cap rate: 6.75%
- Assessed value: $789,630 (on $53.3K NOI)
- True value: $385,185 (on $26K NOI)
- Overvaluation: $404,445 (51%)

### Stratton/Magic Mountain: Seasonal Hospitality Overvaluation

Stratton and Magic Mountain ski areas generate seasonal lodging demand. Properties (inns, condos, rental lodges) are overvalued on peak occupancy assumptions.

A mountain inn (12 rooms, assessed $680,000):
- Lister's assumption: 70% annual occupancy at $120/night = $368,400 gross
- Actual blended occupancy (winter 80%, summer 45%, shoulder 40%): 55% annual = $289,620 gross
- Operating expenses: 40% = $173,772 NOI
- Lister's assumed NOI: $220,000
- Actual NOI: $115,848
- Cap rate: 7% (seasonal)
- Assessed value: $3.14M (if 7% cap on $220K)
- True value: $1.65M (if $115.8K ÷ 7%)
- Overvaluation: $1.49M (48%)

More likely, Lister used cost approach at $680K (replacement cost minus depreciation). True income-based value is $1.65M. But if income approach was used with peak-season assumptions, value is overstated.

### Arlington and Route 7 Retail: Gateway Premium Myth

Properties fronting Route 7 are valued with "New York gateway to Vermont" premium. In reality, Route 7 is a secondary highway; actual commercial traffic is modest.

## The Vermont Grievance Process for Bennington County

### Grand List (April 1) → Lister Grievance (May 1)

Vermont law requires the Grand List to be filed by April 1. You have 30 days to file a written grievance.

**File with:**
- Bennington: City Assessor, (802) 442-1037
- Manchester: Town Listers, (802) 362-1313
- Arlington: Town Listers, (802) 375-6521
- Dorset: Town Listers, (802) 867-2405
- Other towns: Contact town clerk

**Deadline:** May 1 (approximately)

**What to include:**
- Property address and ID number
- Assessed value
- Requested value with justification
- Supporting evidence (comps, income analysis, market data)

### Lister Hearing (June)

Listers schedule hearings by mid-June. Present comparable sales analysis (residential) or income analysis (commercial/seasonal).

### BCA Appeal (14 days after lister decision)

If unsatisfied, appeal to Board of Civil Authority within 14 days. BCA conducts site inspection and hearing. Success rate: 5–12% reductions with solid evidence.

### State Appeal (30 days after BCA decision)

Appeal to Director of Property Valuation and Review if necessary. Success rate: 40–50% with strong evidence.

## Evidence That Wins Grievances in Bennington County

### Residential: Market Correction Evidence

For residential properties, document post-2023 price softening. Manchester properties in particular have corrected 8–15% from 2022–2023 peak.

Gather 3–5 comparable sales within past 12 months. Compare to assessment locked in at higher 2023 valuations.

**Example: Manchester residential, assessed $625K at 2023 peak, now worth $560K**

Comps (Feb–Mar 2026):
1. Similar property, sold Feb 2026 for $555K
2. Similar property, sold Mar 2026 for $570K
3. Similar property, sold Jan 2026 for $545K

Average: $557K

Assessment at $625K is 12.2% high. Request reduction to $560K.

### Commercial: Outlet Premium vs. Reality

For non-outlet Manchester retail, document that your property does not command outlet-level rents.

Gather:
- Actual lease agreements (what rents your tenants pay)
- Comparable retail rents in secondary Manchester locations (broker quotes)
- Comparable retail rents in similar Vermont towns (showing outlet is outlier)
- Actual occupancy rates and tenant quality

If Lister assumed $22/SF and actual leases are $14–$16/SF, you have overvaluation.

### Seasonal: Cap Rate Adjustment

For Stratton/Magic Mountain properties, document actual blended occupancy (weighted by season). Present market evidence of seasonal risk premium (higher cap rates for seasonal properties).

If Lister assumed 70% occupancy and your actual blended occupancy is 55%, you have 21% overvaluation due to occupancy mismatch alone (before cap rate adjustment).

### Downtown Bennington: Comparable Retail Comps

For downtown Bennington commercial, gather comparable retail/office sales from similar New England downtowns showing modest valuations. Downtown Bennington is not a strong market; properties should reflect that reality.
