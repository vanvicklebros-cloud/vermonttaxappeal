## Addison County: Agricultural Heartland and College Town Microcosm

Addison County—Vermont's agricultural engine—stretches across the Champlain Valley from Middlebury (college town, population 6,500) through Bristol, Vergennes, New Haven, and dozens of farming communities. The county's Grand List exceeds $2.8 billion, with median property tax bills around $3,500–$4,400 annually.

Addison County's economy is uniquely tied to agriculture and educational institutions, which creates systematic overvaluation:

- **Middlebury College premium distorts commercial valuations.** The college drives demand for student housing, office space, and retail. Listers often apply Middlebury rates to properties in adjacent towns (Bristol, Cornwall, New Haven) that lack college demand.
- **Dairy economics have collapsed.** Vermont dairy exports are down 40% since 2010. Yet Listers value farms and agricultural properties on historical commodity price assumptions, not current economics.
- **Agritourism assumptions exceed reality.** Farms with orchards, farm stands, corn mazes, and farm stays are overvalued on speculative agritourism income that rarely materializes.
- **Agricultural buildings are overvalued by cost approach.** Specialized structures (creameries, grain storage, milking parlors) are assessed on replacement cost, not actual income generation.
- **Farmland values are artificially inflated.** Farmland in the Champlain Valley is valued partly on speculative development potential, partly on declining agricultural returns. True value is underperforming.

## Residential Overtaxation in Addison County

### Middlebury Residential: College-Town Premium Inflation

Middlebury residential properties are assessed with a college-town premium that has softened post-pandemic. A Middlebury residential property (3-bedroom cape, 1,700 SF, assessed $385,000 in 2024) is overvalued by 8–14%.

Current comparable sales:
- Middlebury near campus: $345–$375K (declining)
- Middlebury near downtown: $365–$395K
- Comparable properties sold Feb–Mar 2026: $340–$370K (average: $355K)

Assessment at $385K is 8.5% high. The premium for Middlebury location is real but not 10%+ above comparable Vermont towns. Student housing demand has moderated; families are migrating to less expensive areas.

### Bristol and Adjacent Towns: Spillover Premium Shouldn't Apply

Bristol, Lincoln, Cornwall, and other Addison towns near Middlebury are assessed on "Middlebury-adjacent" premiums that shouldn't exist. A Bristol residential property (3-bedroom colonial, 1,900 SF, assessed $325,000) is overvalued by 10–15%.

Bristol comps show $265–$295K range. The property assessed at $325K is 10–20% above market. Listers incorrectly apply Middlebury college-town premium to Bristol properties, which lack college demand.

### Rural Addison: Farmland-Residential Hybrid Overvaluation

Rural residential properties on farmland (e.g., farmhouse on 50-acre parcel) are overvalued on "farm potential" assumptions. A rural property (4-bedroom farmhouse, 2,200 SF, on 50 acres, assessed $420,000) should be assessed $340–$380K.

The 50 acres is not generating agricultural income; the property is residential-only. Listers apply agricultural land value multipliers ($3,500–$5,000/acre for prime Champlain Valley farmland) even if the land is not farmed. True value is lower.

## Commercial Property Overtaxation in Addison County

### Middlebury Commercial: College Demand Over-Assumed

Middlebury's downtown commercial district (Main Street, Bakery Lane, Court Street) is valued on assumptions of college employee and student demand. These assumptions have moderated.

A Middlebury downtown retail building (2,500 SF, first floor; 2,500 SF office, second floor):
- Lister's assumption: $18/SF retail (95% occupancy) + $13/SF office (90% occupancy) = $71,250 gross income
- Actual reality: $14/SF retail (70% occupancy) + $10/SF office (80% occupancy) = $35,000 gross income
- Operating expenses: $12,000
- Lister's assumed NOI: $59,250
- Actual NOI: $23,000
- Lister's cap rate: 5.5% (college-town premium, low risk)
- Assessed value: $1.08M
- Market cap rate: 6.5% (mixed-use, college town)
- True value: $354,000
- Overvaluation: $726,000 (67%)

### Agricultural Processing: Creamery and Farm Building Overvaluation

Agricultural buildings (creameries, grain facilities, farm stores, milking parlors) are overvalued because Listers lack recent sales comps and use cost approach based on historic replacement costs.

A creamery facility (6,000 SF, industrial-type, assessed $380,000):
- Replacement cost (if built new): $1.2M
- Age/condition depreciation: –60%
- Assessed value: $480,000

Actual income (from creamery operations or lease to dairy cooperative):
- Annual revenue: $85,000
- Operating expenses: $65,000
- NOI: $20,000
- Market cap rate: 9% (agricultural processing, specialized risk)
- True value: $222,222
- Overvaluation: $257,778 (54%)

### Agritourism Properties: Speculative Income Overvaluation

Farms with orchards, farm stands, corn mazes, or "farm stay" lodging are overvalued on speculative agritourism income.

Example: An orchard property (20 acres, farmstand, apple-picking operation, assessed $350,000):
- Lister's assumption: orchard income $25K + farm stand $40K + agritourism $30K = $95K gross
- Actual income: orchard $18K + farm stand $22K + minimal agritourism $3K = $43K
- Operating expenses (labor, equipment, insurance): $28K
- Lister's assumed NOI: $67K
- Actual NOI: $15K
- Cap rate: 7.5%
- Lister's implied value: $893,000 (if 7.5% cap)
- True value: $200,000 (15K ÷ 7.5%)
- Overvaluation: $693,000 (implies major cost approach + income approach confusion)

More realistically, Lister assessed on cost approach at $350K (land + replacement cost of structures). True income-based value is $200K.

### Vergennes and Rural Town Commercial: Limited Demand

Vergennes is a small commercial hub; Lincoln, New Haven, and other rural towns have minimal commercial demand. Properties are overvalued on assumptions of activity that doesn't materialize.

A Vergennes downtown retail building (1,500 SF, single-tenant, assessed $165,000) is overvalued by 15–20%. Current occupancy is 70%; historical assumptions are 90%+. True value is $125–$140K.

## The Vermont Grievance Process for Addison County

### Grand List (April 1) → Lister Grievance (May 1)

Vermont law requires the Grand List to be filed by April 1. You have 30 days to file a written grievance.

**File with:**
- Middlebury: Town Listers, (802) 388-8100
- Bristol: Town Listers, (802) 453-2410
- Lincoln: Town Listers, (802) 453-2410
- Vergennes: Town Listers, (802) 877-2811
- Other towns: Contact town clerk

**Deadline:** May 1 (approximately)

**What to include:**
- Property address and ID number
- Assessed value
- Requested value with justification
- Supporting evidence (comps, income analysis, farm data)

### Lister Hearing (June)

Listers schedule hearings by mid-June. For agricultural properties, bring Schedule F tax returns, milk production records, commodity price data. For commercial, bring income analysis and rent comparables.

### BCA Appeal (14 days after lister decision)

If unsatisfied, appeal to Board of Civil Authority within 14 days. BCA conducts site inspection and hearing. Success rate: 5–12% reductions with solid evidence.

### State Appeal (30 days after BCA decision)

Appeal to Director of Property Valuation and Review if necessary. Success rate: 40–50% with strong evidence.

## Evidence That Wins Grievances in Addison County

### Agricultural Properties: Farm Income Data

For farms and dairy operations, gather:
- Schedule F (IRS farm tax returns) for past 3 years
- Milk production records and prices (USDA dairy price reports)
- Commodity price data (corn, hay, equipment market value)
- Farm balance sheet (assets, liabilities, equity)

If Lister valued farm on historical dairy prices ($18/cwt) and current prices are $15/cwt (–17%), you have clear overvaluation. Lister's value was based on old assumptions.

**Example: 150-acre dairy farm, assessed $420,000**

Lister's assumption: Farm generates $85K income (on historical milk prices and productivity). Cap rate 6% (agricultural low-risk assumption). Value: $1.42M.

Your farm's actual performance (past 3 years average):
- Milk production revenue: $58K (lower volume, lower prices)
- Crop/hay sales: $8K
- Government programs/insurance: $3K
- Total gross: $69K
- Operating expenses (labor, equipment, utilities, feed): $52K
- NOI: $17K
- Cap rate: 8% (reflecting volatility and dairy downturn)
- True value: $212,500

Assessed value is 3× market value. Request reduction to $220K.

### Agritourism: Actual Income Documentation

For farms with agritourism, document actual income:
- Farm stand sales (register receipts, tax returns)
- Corn maze or orchard-picking revenue
- Farm stay/B&B occupancy (booking records)
- Farm tours or educational programs (revenue)

If Lister assumed $30K agritourism income but you actually generate $5K, you have overvaluation.

### Middlebury Commercial: Comparable Rent Evidence

For Middlebury commercial properties, gather:
- Recent lease agreements (actual rents, not asking)
- Downtown retail vacancy rates (walk Main Street, count empty storefronts)
- Comparable office space rents (college employees, are they accepting lower rents?)
- CoStar data on New England college-town commercial fundamentals

If Lister assumed $18/SF retail and nearby recent leases are $13–$14/SF, you have clear evidence.

### Agricultural Building: Cost Approach vs. Income Reality

For creameries, grain facilities, milking parlors, document:
- Actual income generated (lease payments to farmers, milk processing fees)
- Comparable sales of similar agricultural buildings (rare, but farm real estate databases exist)
- Replacement cost fallacy: a building designed for one specific agricultural purpose has limited alternative use and minimal reproduction value

## Market Context: Why Addison County Grievances Succeed

1. **Dairy Downturn Is Public Knowledge**: Vermont dairy is in structural decline. BCA members understand that farm valuations on pre-downturn prices are wrong.

2. **Middlebury College Demand Is Measured**: Student enrollment is public data. If enrollment is stable or declining, college-town premium should not inflate property values beyond market reality.

3. **Agritourism Rarely Works**: BCA judges understand that farm agritourism is speculative and rarely generates the income Listers assume.

4. **Cost Approach Overvalues Specialized Buildings**: A milking parlor or creamery has no alternative use. Cost approach dramatically overstates value when actual income is minimal.

5. **Bristol and Rural Town Premium Is Indefensible**: Listers cannot justify college-town premium for properties in towns 5–10 miles from Middlebury without college presence.
