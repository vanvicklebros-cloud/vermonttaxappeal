## Why Residential Homeowners Are Overtaxed in Lamoille County

### The Stowe Premium Problem

Stowe's legendary status as a ski destination and summer getaway drives unrealistic residential assessments. Listers apply "Stowe" multipliers to properties based on proximity to the mountain and village—but this premium hasn't reflected actual market conditions since 2022.

**What's happening:**
- Stowe's 2024–2025 reappraisal used inflated comparable sales from 2021–2022 (the peak of pandemic-era second-home speculation)
- Properties listed in $700K–$900K ranges aren't selling; actual sales cluster at $550K–$700K
- Listers refuse to downward-adjust for market correction, instead clinging to "average" prices from 3–4 years ago
- Out-of-state vacation home owners bear unrealistic tax burdens due to this lag

### The Dual Education Tax Rate Impact

Vermont's **homestead education tax rate** (for primary residences) is substantially lower than the **nonhomestead rate** (for vacation homes, rentals, and investment properties). Listers in Stowe systematically misclassify properties as nonhomestead to inflate tax liability:

- A primary residence claimed with proper homestead status should pay 0.72% education tax
- The same property misclassified as nonhomestead pays 1.16% education tax
- In Stowe, this 44 basis point spread translates to $2,200–$3,300 on a $500K home

**Common Lister errors:**
- Requiring excessive documentation for homestead claims
- Delaying homestead certification until tax billing (pushing incorrect assessment into collection)
- Classifying part-time residences as nonhomestead when statutory homestead applies
- Applying nonhomestead rates retroactively for prior years without notice

### 2025 Statewide Adjustment Miscalculations

The 2025 Statewide Adjustment fundamentally changed how Vermont assesses residential properties, shifting from sales-based approaches to income-based models in some categories. Town Listers in Lamoille have misapplied this new formula:

- Properties reassessed at inflated "estimated income" values rather than recent arm's-length sales
- Vacation rental properties valued using unrealistic occupancy rates (85% vs. actual 60% for similar Stowe properties)
- Primary residences downward-adjusted insufficiently despite documented market softness
- No transparency in how the Statewide Adjustment adjusted individual property factors

## Why Commercial Properties Are Overvalued in Lamoille County

### The Hospitality Income Capitalization Error

Commercial hospitality properties (hotels, inns, restaurants, ski lodges) in Lamoille are systematically overvalued using flawed income capitalization approaches. Listers apply overly aggressive assumptions:

**The error:**
1. **Overstated gross revenue** – Listers use peak-season revenue (Jan–Feb ski season, Aug–Sept foliage) as annual baseline, ignoring shoulder and off-season reality
2. **Inflated operating expense estimates** – Using national chain standards (40% of revenue) rather than actual Stowe operator expenses (55%+)
3. **Inappropriate cap rate** – Using 5% cap rates (appropriate for stabilized urban properties) instead of 8%–9% for seasonal, management-dependent mountain lodging
4. **Ignored obsolescence** – Older properties with deferred maintenance assessed "as if" newly renovated

**Real-world example:** A 50-room inn with $1.8M actual gross revenue, 55% operating expenses, and documented seasonal volatility:
- **Lister calculation:** $1.8M × 45% NOI ÷ 5% cap = $16.2M valuation
- **Market reality:** $1.2M × 45% NOI ÷ 8% cap = $6.75M valuation
- **Overvaluation:** $9.45M (140% above market)

This yields property tax liability 2–3x the property's actual annual cash flow.

### Restaurant and Retail Overvaluation

Lamoille's Route 100 corridor restaurants and retail suffer similar income approach errors:

- **Comparable sales ignored** – Listers avoid recent sales of similar Stowe/Morrisville restaurants, instead using national retail cap rate matrices
- **Revenue timing distortions** – Winter season revenue treated as annualized basis (seasonal ski tourists, foliage traffic) without regard to summer/shoulder-season declines
- **Expense under-allocation** – Ski-town labor costs 25–35% above national averages, utilities spike in winter, but Listers use standard retail expense assumptions
- **Tenant risk ignored** – Seasonal restaurant turnover (avg. 3–4 years in Stowe), but Listers assess "stabilized" properties as if occupancy is permanent

### Ski Property Valuation Distortions

Vacation properties and second homes used by ski families face inflated assessments from speculative 2021–2022 comparable sales:

- Properties marketed as "ski-in/ski-out" assessed at premiums that exist only during peak season
- Maintenance costs, avalanche risk, slope-side infrastructure costs ignored in valuation models
- Listers rely on resort marketing materials instead of actual paired sales data
- Properties are valued "as if" owner-occupied despite being short-term rentals or investment properties

## The Vermont Grievance & Appeal Process: Your Pathway to a Reduction

Vermont law (32 V.S.A. § 4111) guarantees all property owners the right to challenge Grand List assessments. Here's the precise timeline and process for Lamoille County:

### Step 1: The Grand List Filing (April 1)

Your town Listers file the completed Grand List with the Director of Property Valuation and Review by April 1. The Grand List is the official record of all property assessments for that town and tax year. You're entitled to inspect the Grand List at your town office and review your assessment details.

### Step 2: Grievance Filing (30 days from Grand List)

Within 30 days of the Grand List filing, you must file a **written grievance** with your town Listers. The deadline varies by town:
- **Stowe:** May 5 (2026)
- **Morrisville:** May 8 (2026)
- **Johnson:** May 6 (2026)
- Other Lamoille towns follow similar mid-May timelines

**Your grievance must include:**
- Property identification (Grand List number, street address)
- Statement of alleged error(s) in valuation or classification
- Specific evidence supporting your position (comps, condition reports, income documentation)
- Requested assessed value

**Critical:** We prepare and file all grievance documents. You simply provide property details and supporting evidence (see "Winning Grievance Evidence" section below).

### Step 3: Listers' Hearing (60–90 days after grievance)

Town Listers schedule a formal hearing to consider your grievance. In Lamoille, most Lister boards meet in May–June. The hearing is your opportunity to present evidence directly; Listers must give you a fair hearing and cannot dismiss frivolous evidence out of hand.

**What happens:**
- You present your case (we present for you if desired)
- Listers ask questions and review your documentation
- Listers must provide written findings explaining their decision
- If they adjust your assessment downward, it's modified on the Grand List
- If they deny your grievance, you proceed to BCA appeal

### Step 4: Board of Civil Authority (BCA) Appeal (14 days after denial)

If Listers deny your grievance, you have **14 days** to appeal to your town's **Board of Civil Authority** (select board + town clerk, or in some towns a dedicated BCA). The BCA is Lamoille's first appellate body.

**BCA standard:** Listers' assessment is presumed correct; you must prove by clear and convincing evidence that their valuation is wrong.

**BCA timeline:**
- File appeal notice within 14 days of Listers' denial
- BCA schedules hearing (typically 30–45 days later)
- You present evidence; Listers defend their valuation
- BCA issues decision within 30 days

### Step 5: Director of Property Valuation and Review (State Appeal)

If BCA denies your appeal, you may appeal to Vermont's **Director of Property Valuation and Review** (state level). This is your final administrative appeal before litigation.

**State appeal standard:** De novo review; Director weighs evidence independently without deference to Listers' or BCA's findings.

**Filing requirements:**
- File within 30 days of BCA decision
- Submit detailed brief with all evidence
- Director issues decision within 60 days
- Decision is final for assessment purposes

### Step 6: Superior Court (If Necessary)

If the Director denies your appeal, you may petition Superior Court for judicial review. This is rarely necessary for properly supported grievances.

## Winning Grievance Evidence: What Persuades Listers & the BCA

### Comparable Sales Analysis (Most Powerful)

**Residential properties:** Find 3–5 recent arm's-length sales of similar properties in the same neighborhood, sold within 12 months. Adjust for differences:
- Square footage, age, condition
- Lot size, views, slope, access
- Renovation recency, mechanical systems

**For Stowe residential:** Recent sales under asking price are your gold. A property listed at $650K but sold at $575K directly contradicts a $620K assessment.

**Commercial properties (hospitality, retail):** Sales of comparable hotels, inns, or restaurants are rare, but when available, they're decisive. Example:
- A 30-room Stowe inn sold for $4.2M (2024)
- Your 32-room inn is assessed at $6.8M
- This recent comparable is prima facie evidence of overvaluation

### Income and Expense Documentation

**For commercial properties using income approach:**
- 2–3 years of actual tax returns (Schedule C, Form 1040-C, IRS Form 1065)
- Detailed P&L statements showing gross revenue, operating expenses, net operating income
- Bank statements documenting actual revenue deposits
- Payroll records showing actual labor costs
- Utility bills showing actual operating costs

**For seasonal hospitality (ski lodges, inns):**
- Actual booking data showing occupancy rates (many Stowe properties run 55–70%, not the 85% Listers assume)
- Documented seasonal revenue variation (highlight that February ≠ July)
- Deferred maintenance lists and contractor estimates
- Management company agreements showing actual fees paid

**For vacation rental properties:**
- Airbnb/VRBO historical data showing actual rental revenue, occupancy, and seasonal variation
- Proof that property is short-term rental (not primary residence) and thus properly nonhomestead-classified
- Turnover costs, cleaning fees, property management fees reducing net income

### Professional Appraisals and Valuations

A certified professional appraisal from an MAI appraiser (with Vermont experience) carries tremendous weight. Cost: $1,500–$3,500. The appraiser:
- Selects comparable sales specific to the property type and location
- Adjusts comps for differences
- Applies appropriate valuation methodology (sales approach, income approach, or cost approach)
- Concludes with a defensible market value conclusion

**Critical:** Ensure the appraiser is experienced with ski-town valuations, seasonal adjustments, and Vermont Grand List standards.

### Condition and Physical Deficiency Reports

Properties with deferred maintenance, obsolescence, or physical defects are often overvalued because Listers assess "as if" properties are in average condition.

**Document:**
- Roof age and condition (cost to replace)
- HVAC system age and performance
- Plumbing, electrical, foundation issues
- Structural damage or settling
- Environmental issues (radon, mold, wetlands)
- Contractor quotes for repairs needed

Example: A $550K home with a $75K roof replacement needed and $30K foundation work is worth $445K, not $550K.

### Lister Methodology Errors (Advanced)

If Listers applied flawed valuation methodology, document the error:

**For residential (2025 Statewide Adjustment):**
- Prove that Listers misapplied the income adjustment factors
- Show that the property's recent sales price contradicts the assessed value
- Demonstrate that comparable properties' sales-to-assessment ratios differ significantly from your property

**For commercial (income capitalization):**
- Prove that Listers overstated gross revenue (submit actual tax returns)
- Prove that Listers understated operating expenses (submit actual P&Ls)
- Show that the cap rate used is inappropriate for seasonal, management-dependent properties
- Demonstrate that comparable properties sell at much lower cap rates

## Lamoille County Market Dynamics: Specific Challenges & Opportunities

### The Stowe Liquidity Problem

Stowe properties are highly illiquid. Most residences remain unsold for 6–12 months despite accurate pricing. This illiquidity inflates Listers' assessments because they treat listed prices as "asking price evidence" rather than recognizing that actual sales occur at 15–20% discounts.

**Opportunity:** If your property has been listed 6+ months without sale, this is powerful evidence of overvaluation. Document the listing history; it directly contradicts any comparable sales argument Listers advance.

### Ski Season Dependence Overlays

Lamoille's economy—and property values—are entirely dependent on ski season conditions (Dec–Feb). A poor snow year or warm winter dramatically depresses property sales, rents, and occupancy. Yet Listers assess properties assuming average conditions.

**If your commercial property suffered from poor 2024–2025 ski season**, this is recent evidence that your income and overall valuation are depressed. NOI for a Stowe restaurant in a poor ski year is 40–50% below a good year.

### Route 100 Corridor Saturation

Morrisville and the Route 100 strip have experienced retail and hospitality saturation. New businesses struggle; existing ones see declining profitability. Yet Listers continue valuing corridor commercial properties using optimistic revenue assumptions from 2021–2022.

**Opportunity:** If you own Route 100 retail or hospitality, recent years' declining revenues are your strongest evidence. Trend the income down 3 years; BCA and state Director recognize that recoveries are not guaranteed.

### Vacation Rental Market Compression

The short-term rental market (Airbnb, VRBO) in Lamoille is compressed. Oversupply of vacation properties, rising cleaning and management costs, and platform fee increases (Airbnb now takes 16–18%) have depressed rental income. Properties that grossed $45K/year 3 years ago now gross $32K.

**For vacation property owners:** This income compression is direct evidence that your property's current assessment—based on prior-year income—is overstated. Provide 3 years of rental income trends.
