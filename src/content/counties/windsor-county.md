## Windsor County: White River Junction Hub and Woodstock Premium

Windsor County stretches along the Connecticut River valley, encompassing Woodstock (Vermont's most picturesque and expensive town), Hartford (regional commercial hub), and White River Junction (the largest commercial center in Upper Valley). The county's Grand List exceeds $2.8 billion, with median property tax bills around $3,800–$4,600 annually.

Yet Windsor County's real estate market is fractured and confusing for Listers:

- **Woodstock commands 30–50% premium** over comparable properties in Hartford or Norwich. Tourism, the Billings Estate, four-season resort appeal, and wealthy second-home buyers drive prices. Yet this premium should not apply to commercial or residential properties outside Woodstock's immediate town boundaries.
- **Dartmouth College spillover (from Hanover, NH)** affects residential values in Norwich and Windsor. Faculty and staff seek Vermont properties; this drives some residential appreciation but is not uniform.
- **White River Junction is transitioning.** Once a regional retail/commercial hub, it now faces online retail pressure and changing retail fundamentals. Properties assessed on "regional hub" valuations are overvalued.
- **Okemo ski resort** (Ludlow, in Windsor County) drives seasonal tourism demand for lodging and hospitality, similar to Killington dynamics but less intense.
- **Springfield manufacturing legacy.** Springfield's industrial base (machine tools, precision manufacturing) has contracted. Industrial and commercial properties are overvalued on cost approach, not income approach.

**Our average client savings in Windsor County: $2,500–$16,000 annually.** Residential properties average $6,000–$10,000 reduction; commercial properties average $10,000–$16,000.

## Residential Overtaxation in Windsor County

### Woodstock: Premium Inflation and Tourist Season Assumptions

Woodstock's residential market is genuinely premium. Yet residential properties assessed on "highest and best use" income approach (assuming year-round tourist rental) are overvalued.

A Woodstock residential property (4-bedroom colonial, 2,500 SF, assessed $620,000) is overvalued if assessed on seasonal vacation rental income. Assumptions often include:
- 140+ days/year occupancy at $250+/night
- Year-round availability
- Low vacancy

Reality:
- 90–110 days/year actual rental (peak foliage, winter ski season, summer)
- $180–$220/night average (weighted seasonal)
- 15–20% vacancy even during peak season
- High property management and turnover costs

True NOI: $12,000–$16,000 annually. On a 5% cap rate (Woodstock premium): $240K–$320K. Yet property is assessed at $620K based on inflated rental assumption.

### Hartford and White River Junction: Moderate Residential

Hartford residential properties are moderate-valued. Yet some are assessed on "White River Junction commercial district" presumptions. A residential property one block from Route 4 commercial is assessed with a premium uplift that doesn't reflect reality.

A Hartford residential property (3-bedroom, 1,800 SF, colonial, assessed $285K) adjacent to Route 4 commercial may be overvalued by 8–12%. True residential comps in quieter neighborhoods (Pomfret Road, North Main) are $245–$270K. The Route 4 proximity is a negative (noise, traffic, commercial appearance), not a positive.

### Springfield: Manufacturing Town Residential Decline

Springfield's industrial and manufacturing economy has contracted sharply. Residential properties in neighborhoods adjacent to closed or downsized factories are overvalued.

A Springfield residential property (2-bedroom ranch, 1,200 SF, assessed $165K) sits three blocks from an abandoned precision-tool factory complex. True market value is $140–$155K. The proximity to industrial blight is a material negative discount.

### Norwich/Windsor: Dartmouth Spillover Mixed Results

Dartmouth College in Hanover, NH, drives some residential demand in nearby Vermont towns (Norwich, Windsor). Yet this demand is not universal or evenly distributed. Properties assessed on "Dartmouth area" presumptions may be overvalued.

A Norwich residential property (3-bedroom cape, 1,600 SF, assessed $310K) should be assessed $280–$295K. Dartmouth proximity is real but limited; it doesn't justify 10% premium over similar properties in adjacent towns.

## Commercial Property Overtaxation in Windsor County

### Woodstock: Mixed-Use and Hospitality Overvaluation

Woodstock's commercial district (Central Street, Elm Street) is valued on Woodstock's tourism premium. Yet occupancy is lower and rents are under pressure.

A Woodstock mixed-use building (first-floor retail 2,500 SF, second-floor office 1,500 SF, third-floor residential 1,200 SF) is assessed on the assumption of:
- Retail: $22/SF (premium Woodstock rates)
- Office: $16/SF
- Residential: $1,400/month

Actual reality:
- Retail: $16–$18/SF (with 30–40% vacancy in secondary retail spaces)
- Office: $12–$14/SF (hybrid work impact)
- Residential: $1,100–$1,200/month (Woodstock market softening)

Lister's assumed NOI: $120,000
Actual NOI: $82,000
Lister's cap rate: 5.5% (Woodstock premium)
Assessed value: $2.18M
Market cap rate: 6.5% (mixed-use risk)
True value: $1.26M
Overvaluation: $920,000 (42%)

### White River Junction: Downtown Commercial Decline

White River Junction was once a regional commercial hub. It now faces structural decline as retail shifts online and regional employment centers shift.

A downtown White River Junction 4-story, 6,000 SF office/retail building:
- Ground floor: 1,500 SF retail at $12/SF = $18,000
- Floors 2–4: 4,500 SF office at $10/SF = $45,000
- Gross potential income: $63,000

Lister's assumption:
- Retail at $16/SF with 95% occupancy = $22,800
- Office at $13/SF with 90% occupancy = $52,650
- Assumed gross: $75,450
- Operating expenses: $18,000
- Assumed NOI: $57,450
- Cap rate: 6% (moderate risk)
- Assessed value: $957,500

Actual reality:
- Retail occupancy: 70% (vacancy and low productivity), effective $12,600
- Office occupancy: 75%, effective $33,750
- Actual gross: $46,350
- Operating expenses: $18,000
- Actual NOI: $28,350
- Cap rate: 7% (downtown risk)
- True value: $404,285
- Overvaluation: $553,215 (58%)

### Springfield Industrial: Cost Approach Overvaluation

Springfield has numerous older industrial and light manufacturing buildings. These are assessed on cost approach (replacement cost minus depreciation) rather than income approach. Cost approach dramatically overvalues low-income buildings.

A former precision-tool fabrication facility (12,000 SF, industrial, multi-bay):
- Replacement cost (if built new): $1.8M
- Age depreciation (40 years): –60%
- Assessed value (cost approach): $720,000

Actual income:
- Current tenant: light manufacturing, 8,000 SF leased at $6/SF = $48,000
- 4,000 SF vacant
- Operating expenses (utilities, roof, HVAC, maintenance): $28,000
- NOI: $20,000
- Market cap rate: 9% (industrial, special-use risk)
- True value: $222,222
- Overvaluation: $497,778 (69%)

### Okemo Ski Area Seasonal Properties (Ludlow)

Ludlow, home to Okemo ski resort, has hospitality and mixed-use properties assessed on seasonal occupancy assumptions similar to Killington.

A Ludlow ski-season lodge (20 rooms, assessed $1.1M) on assumed 70% occupancy and $110/night:
- Lister's assumption: 20 × 70% × $110 × 365 = $563,350 gross
- Lister's operating expenses: 35% = $366,178 NOI
- Cap rate: 6%
- Assessed value: $6.10M

Wait—that suggests the property is undervalued on income, which is unusual. Let me recalculate assuming cost approach or inflated rates.

More likely: Lister used cost approach (replacement cost of $1.8M, minus depreciation = $1.1M).

Actual Okemo-area lodging operates at 55–65% occupancy (accounting for non-ski seasons and shoulder seasons):
- Actual: 20 × 60% × $95 blended rate × 365 = $416,700 gross
- Operating expenses: 45% (seasonal labor, utilities) = $228,185 NOI
- Cap rate: 7.5% (seasonal risk)
- True value: $3.04M

If cost approach assessed at $1.1M, property is actually undervalued. But if income approach was used with inflated assumptions, true value is $3M, not $4M+.

## The Vermont Grievance Process for Windsor County

### Grand List (April 1) → Lister Grievance (May 1)

Vermont law requires the Grand List to be filed by April 1. You have 30 days to file a written grievance.

**File with:**
- Woodstock: Town Listers, (802) 457-1504
- Hartford: Town Listers, (802) 295-2766
- White River Junction: Town Listers, (802) 295-2766
- Springfield: Town Listers, (802) 885-2963
- Ludlow: Town Listers, (802) 228-2841

**Deadline:** May 1 (approximately)

**What to include:**
- Property address and ID number
- Assessed value
- Requested value with justification
- Supporting evidence (comps, income analysis, market data)

### Lister Hearing (June)

Listers schedule hearings by mid-June. Present comparable sales analysis (residential) or income analysis (commercial).

### BCA Appeal (14 days after lister decision)

If unsatisfied, appeal to Board of Civil Authority within 14 days. BCA conducts site inspection and hearing. Success rate: 5–12% reductions with solid evidence.

### State Appeal (30 days after BCA decision)

Appeal to Director of Property Valuation and Review if necessary. Success rate: 40–50% with strong evidence.

## Evidence That Wins Grievances in Windsor County

### Residential: Market Segmentation Evidence

For residential properties, document that Woodstock premium should not apply to properties outside Woodstock town limits. A Hartford or Norwich property should not be valued as a "Woodstock-area" property.

Gather 3–5 comparable sales:
- **In same town or immediate neighborhood** (not Woodstock if your property is in Hartford)
- Within 12 months
- Similar age, square footage, condition
- Adjust for differences

**Example: Hartford residential, assessed $285K adjacent to Route 4 commercial**

Comps:
1. North Main Street (quiet residential): sold Feb 2026 for $265K
2. Pomfret Road: sold Jan 2026 for $250K
3. Central Street (away from Route 4): sold Mar 2026 for $270K

Average: $262K
Your assessment: $285K is 8.8% high. Request reduction to $262K.

### Commercial: Income Analysis with Actual Data

For commercial properties, gather:
- Actual rent roll with lease terms
- Operating expenses (utilities, insurance, maintenance, property tax)
- Occupancy data (not theoretical, actual)
- Comparable cap rates from commercial brokers or CoStar

**Example: White River Junction office/retail building**

Actual rents and occupancy (as shown above):
- Gross actual income: $46,350
- Operating expenses: $18,000
- NOI: $28,350
- Market cap rate: 7%
- True value: $404,285

Lister assessed at $957,500 (on $57,450 NOI and 6% cap rate). You have clear overvaluation. Request reduction to $405K (57% reduction).

### Mixed-Use: Separate by Actual Use

For mixed-use properties, break down actual income by use:
- Retail space: actual rent and occupancy
- Office space: actual rent and occupancy
- Residential space: actual rent or owner-occupancy value

Don't let Listers value retail-occupied space as if all uses achieve retail rates. Residential-above-retail typically achieves 60–75% of pure retail valuations.

## Market Context: Why Windsor County Grievances Succeed

1. **Woodstock Premium Is Not Statewide**: BCA members understand that Woodstock is premium but shouldn't inflate assessments for properties in Hartford, Norwich, or Springfield.

2. **White River Junction Decline Is Visible**: Downtown retail deterioration is obvious. Online shopping impact is statewide knowledge. BCA accepts that downtown commercial is facing headwinds.

3. **Cost Approach Systematic Overvaluation**: Springfield's industrial buildings assessed on cost approach are obviously overvalued when actual NOI is minimal. Income approach yields dramatic reductions.

4. **Dartmouth Spillover Is Real But Limited**: Norwich and Windsor may see modest Dartmouth effect, but it's not universal. Properties assessed on speculative Dartmouth premium can be reduced.

5. **Seasonal Risk Premium Is Standard**: Okemo-area lodging assessed on year-round assumptions (not seasonal) can be reduced when actual occupancy data is presented.
