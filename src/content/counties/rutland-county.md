## Rutland County: Ski Resort Economy and Downtown Decline

Rutland County—Vermont's southwest hub—stretches from the city of Rutland (population 12,600) through Killington's world-class ski resort, Castleton's historic village, and Fair Haven's marble-industry legacy. The county's Grand List exceeds $3.1 billion, with median property tax bills around $3,800–$5,200 annually.

Yet Rutland County's economy presents contradictory dynamics that confuse property assessments:

- **Killington ski resort dominates.** Peak season (winter, leaf season) drives property values and tourism. Off-season, the area deflates economically and in demand.
- **Rutland City downtown is in structural decline.** Retail has fled; commercial properties sit vacant or underoccupied; residential neighborhoods have stagnated.
- **Marble industry legacy.** Fair Haven and Castleton have marble quarries, cutting mills, and artistic businesses tied to marble. These are specialty markets with volatile income.
- **Route 4 corridor (Killington approach).** Commercial properties along Route 4 are valued as if ski season demand is year-round, but true occupancy is highly seasonal.
- **Residential lakefront (Bomoseen, Carving Pond).** These areas have premium properties assessed on peak seasonal demand; actual year-round occupancy is much lower.

**Our average client savings in Rutland County: $2,500–$15,000 annually.** Seasonal resort properties average $8,000–$15,000 reductions; Rutland City residential properties average $4,000–$7,000.

## Residential Overtaxation in Rutland County

### Killington-Area Seasonal Homes: Occupancy Overestimation

Properties in and around Killington village (including Woodstock, Quechee, Bridgewater, and Felchville) are assessed on the assumption of year-round occupancy and peak rental income. Reality is sharply different.

A Killington-area 4-bedroom vacation home assessed at $520,000 on seasonal rental income is overvalued by 15–25%. Assumptions:
- Occupied 180+ days/year (peak-season thinking)
- $200+/night average rent
- High-season vs. low-season differential ignored

Actual reality:
- Occupied 90–120 days/year (ski season December–March plus summer spillover)
- Winter rates: $150–$180/night; summer rates: $90–$120/night; shoulder season: $80–$110/night
- 20–25% vacancy even during peak season

A realistic valuation uses 100 days at avg $140/night = $14,000 gross income, not $36,000. After property expenses, insurance, management (25–30% of rent), and maintenance reserves, NOI is 25–35% of gross rent, or $3,500–$4,900 annually. On a 6% cap rate, value is $58,333–$81,667. Yet properties are assessed at $400–$550K based on unrealistic seasonal assumptions.

### Rutland City Residential: Urban Decay Discount

Rutland City's downtown and central neighborhoods have experienced measurable decline since 2015. Yet residential properties are assessed on valuations from 2017–2020, before the acceleration of downtown blight.

A central Rutland residential property (3-bedroom, 1.5-bath, 1,800 SF, 1950s colonial) assessed at $210,000 in 2023 is now worth $165–$185K. Why?

- Downtown commercial vacancies increased 35% since 2020
- Property tax rate increased 12% (forcing price-sensitive buyers away)
- Crime perception increased (actual crime modest, but perception matters)
- School enrollment declined, affecting family appeal

Comparable sales in similar Rutland neighborhoods (Duffy Street, Carver Street) show $160–$190K range. Assessment at $210K is 10–15% high.

### Fair Haven and Castleton: Marble-Dependent Neighborhoods

Fair Haven and Castleton have historically relied on marble quarrying and artistic marble work. As this industry has contracted, residential neighborhoods have become less desirable. Properties assessed on historical marble-economy fundamentals are overvalued.

A Fair Haven residential property (Victorian, 2,200 SF, 3 bed/2 bath, assessed $280K) should be assessed $240–$260K. The marble industry premium (which once justified higher valuations) no longer exists.

### Bomoseen Lake Seasonal Premium Inflation

Bomoseen Lake is the county's premier residential/seasonal market. Lakefront properties have genuine premium, but Listers overestimate seasonal rental income and occupancy, applying excessive income approach valuations.

A lakefront camp (2-bedroom cottage, seasonal) assessed on 120 days at $180/night = $21,600 income is overstating reality. True seasonal market rent is $110–$140/night, 80–100 days actual occupancy (accounting for vacancy and shoulder-season softness) = $8,800–$14,000. After expenses, NOI is $2,500–$4,500. At 6% cap, value is $41,667–$75,000, not $250K.

## Commercial Property Overtaxation in Rutland County

### Killington Resort and Route 4 Corridor: Seasonal Overvaluation

Commercial properties on Route 4 (approach to Killington) are valued as if ski season demand is year-round. Hotels, restaurants, retail shops, and mixed-use buildings face dramatic seasonal occupancy swings:
- Winter (Dec–Mar): 80–90% occupancy
- Summer (Jun–Aug): 50–65% occupancy
- Shoulder (Apr–May, Sep–Nov): 30–45% occupancy
- Blended annual: 55–60%

Yet Listers often assume 75–80% average occupancy.

**Example: Route 4 motel (24 rooms, 3-story)**
- Lister's assumption: 75% occupancy × 24 rooms × $110/night × 365 = $726,750 gross
- Actual: 60% occupancy × 24 rooms × $110 winter + $80 summer blend = $514,320 gross
- Operating expenses (labor, utilities, housekeeping, maintenance): $290,000
- Lister's assumed NOI: $436,750
- Actual NOI: $224,320
- Lister's cap rate: 6.5%
- Assessed value: $671,923
- Market cap rate: 7.5% (seasonal risk)
- True value: $299,093
- Overvaluation: $372,830 (56%)

### Rutland City Downtown: Cost Approach Overvaluation

Rutland City's downtown has numerous older commercial buildings (1950s–1980s multi-story office/retail). These are assessed using cost approach (replacement cost minus depreciation), not income approach. Cost approach systematically overvalues low-income buildings.

A downtown Rutland 8,000 SF, 4-story office/retail building:
- Replacement cost: $1.2M
- Age depreciation (35 years): –50%
- Assessed value: $600,000

Actual income:
- Gross potential rent: $96,000 (assuming $12/SF with 80% occupancy)
- Operating expenses: $24,000
- NOI: $72,000
- Market cap rate: 8% (downtown risk, lower occupancy)
- True value: $900,000

In this case, cost approach undervalues. But if replacement cost was $1.8M:
- Assessed value (cost approach): $900,000
- True value (income approach): $72,000 ÷ 0.08 = $900,000

The problem is when replacement cost is inflated or operating conditions have deteriorated post-construction. A 1970s building assessed at replacement cost minus standard depreciation, but now generating only 50–60% of design-rent income, is overvalued.

### Fair Haven and Castleton: Marble Industry Properties

Fair Haven's marble quarries, cutting mills, and fabrication shops have contracted 70–80% since 1990. Properties assessed on historical marble-economy fundamentals are dramatically overvalued.

A marble cutting mill (8,000 SF, industrial, assessed $320,000) now generates only $45,000 gross revenue (15 employees, specialized work). Operating expenses: $35,000. NOI: $10,000. Market cap rate: 9% (industrial, specialized). True value: $111,111. Assessed value is 3× market reality.

## The Vermont Grievance Process for Rutland County

### Grand List (April 1) → Lister Grievance (May 1)

Vermont law requires the Grand List to be filed by April 1. You have 30 days to file a written grievance.

**File with:**
- Rutland City: City Assessor, (802) 775-0133
- Killington: Town Listers, (802) 422-3736
- Castleton: Town Listers, (802) 468-2213
- Fair Haven: Town Listers, (802) 265-3626

**Deadline:** May 1 (approximately)

**What to include:**
- Property address and ID number
- Assessed value
- Requested value with justification
- Supporting evidence (comps, income analysis, market data)

### Lister Hearing (June)

Listers schedule hearings by mid-June. Present comparable sales analysis (residential) or income analysis (commercial/seasonal properties).

### BCA Appeal (14 days after lister decision)

If unsatisfied, appeal to Board of Civil Authority within 14 days. BCA conducts site inspection and hearing. Success rate: 5–12% reductions with solid evidence.

### State Appeal (30 days after BCA decision)

Appeal to Director of Property Valuation and Review if necessary. Success rate: 40–50% with strong evidence.

## Evidence That Wins Grievances in Rutland County

### Seasonal Residential: Occupancy and Rental Rate Documentation

For seasonal vacation properties, gather:
- Booking records or rental history (Airbnb, VRBO, property manager records)
- Actual occupancy data (nights booked, no-shows, cancellations)
- Actual nightly rates (not advertised, but what you charged)
- Seasonal breakdown (peak vs. shoulder vs. off-season)
- Operating expenses (property tax, insurance, maintenance, management fees)

**Example: Killington vacation home (4 bed, 2 bath)**

Your actual 2024–2025 data:
- Winter (Dec–Mar): 92 nights booked at $165/night = $15,180
- Summer (Jun–Aug): 65 nights booked at $95/night = $6,175
- Shoulder (Apr–May, Sep–Nov): 45 nights booked at $85/night = $3,825
- **Total: 202 nights, $25,180 gross annual rent**
- Operating expenses (property mgmt 25%, insurance, property tax share): $8,050
- **NOI: $17,130**
- Market cap rate: 6.5% (seasonal vacation property)
- **True value: $263,538**

If assessed at $420,000 (on Lister's assumption of 250+ nights at higher rates), you have clear overvaluation. Request reduction to $265K.

### Seasonal Commercial: Cap Rate Adjustment for Risk

For seasonal hotels, restaurants, and resorts, present:
- Actual occupancy data for full year (not just peak season)
- Blended average daily rate (weighted by season)
- Year-round operating expenses
- Comparable properties' cap rates from CoStar or commercial brokers
- Market evidence of seasonal risk premium

**Example: Route 4 motel (actual occupancy 60%, not 75%)**

Lister valued on 75% occupancy, 6.5% cap rate.
You present actual 60% occupancy, blended $90/night rate (weighted seasonal), year-round expenses.

NOI from actual: $224K (as calculated above)
Market cap rate for seasonal: 7.5–8% (reflecting occupancy volatility and seasonal risk)
True value: $224K ÷ 0.075 = $2.99M

If Lister assessed at $3.6M (on higher occupancy/cap rate assumptions), you have 17% overvaluation.

### Marble Industry Decline Narrative

For Fair Haven and Castleton commercial properties tied to marble, document:
- Marble industry employment declines (Vermont Department of Labor, company records)
- Production cutbacks at Rock of Ages and competitors
- Specialized market limitations (marble fabrication has limited buyer pool)
- Income trend data showing declining NOI over 5–10 years

This narrative helps the BCA understand why cap rates should be higher (increased risk) and why income multiples should be lower (specialized market).

### Rutland City Downtown: Comparable Sales with Adjusted Rents

For downtown Rutland office/retail buildings, gather:
- Recent sales of similar downtown buildings in Rutland or comparable Vermont cities
- Current occupancy rates in downtown Rutland (higher than historical; deteriorating)
- Actual lease rates (not asking, but achieved rents)
- Comparison to other downtowns (Montpelier, Barre, Bennington) showing Rutland underperformance

## Market Context: Why Rutland County Grievances Succeed

1. **Seasonal Demand Volatility Is Measurable**: Ski resort dependence creates documented occupancy swings. Listers cannot ignore actual booking data if presented professionally.

2. **Rutland City Decline Is Visible**: Downtown blight is obvious. BCA members see vacant storefronts, deteriorating properties, and reduced foot traffic. Assessments must account for this.

3. **Marble Industry Cutbacks Are Public**: Rock of Ages production declines are documented. Industrial properties tied to marble cannot be valued on historical fundamentals.

4. **Cost Approach Systematic Overvaluation**: Vermont's Director and BCA judges understand cost approach biases. Evidence showing income-approach undervaluation is persuasive.

5. **Seasonal Risk Premium Is Appraisal Standard**: Professional appraisers apply 50–200 bps cap rate premium for seasonal properties. Listers applying standard cap rates to seasonal properties are violating appraisal standards.
