## Washington County: State Capital Stagnation and Industrial Legacy

Washington County sits at Vermont's geographic center. Its economy has long revolved around Montpelier (state capital, population 8,000) and Barre (granite industry hub, population 7,500), with smaller towns like Waterbury, Plainfield, Berlin, and Orange completing the economic landscape. The county's Grand List exceeds $4.2 billion, with median property tax bills around $4,200–$5,600 annually.

Yet Washington County faces unique economic headwinds that make property valuations particularly vulnerable to overassessment:

- **State government employment is stalled.** Vermont's budget crises have frozen hiring at key agencies. UVM in neighboring Chittenden drives Chittenden wages; state jobs in Montpelier are modest and non-growing.
- **Barre granite industry is in terminal decline.** Rock of Ages and smaller quarries operate at a fraction of historical capacity. Properties tied to granite—quarries, cutting mills, fabrication shops—are worth far less than their cost basis from decades past.
- **Waterbury's resort economy (Cold Hollow Cider Mill, tourism) is volatile.** Seasonal tourism fluctuates wildly; property assessments have not adjusted downward to match volatile income.
- **NECI (New England Culinary Institute) closed in 2020.** Its Montpelier campus generated student housing demand, property rentals, and local employment. That anchor is gone, leaving assessed values that reflect pre-closure demand.
- **Flood risk from Mad River increases every year**, reducing desirability and value in Waterbury and Waitsfield neighborhoods.

**Our average client savings in Washington County: $3,000–$18,000 annually.** Residential homes average $6,500 reduction; commercial/industrial properties average $12,000–$18,000 due to larger absolute overvaluations.

## Residential Overtaxation in Washington County

### Montpelier: State Capital with Stalled Growth

Montpelier is Vermont's smallest state capital, with a frozen state payroll and declining downtown vibrancy. Yet assessment rolls treat residential properties as if the state government is growing. Assessments have not adjusted downward despite flat employment.

A typical Montpelier residential property (1970s colonial, 2,100 SF, 3 bed/2 bath) assessed at $345,000 in 2025 is overvalued by 8–12%. Current market comps in neighborhoods like Hubbard Park and the North Street area are selling for $300–$325K. Listers used 2023 sales (when state optimism was higher) as comparables, without accounting for 2026 market reality.

**Assessment lag problem:**
- 2023 Montpelier median home sale: $350K
- 2026 Montpelier median home sale: $318K
- Median depreciation: 9.1%
- Yet assessment of typical home has increased 3–5%, not decreased

The cause: listers reappraise on 5–7 year cycles. Montpelier's last reappraisal (2022–2023) used strong sales data. Now, 3 years later, values have declined, but assessments haven't caught up.

### Barre: Granite Town in Decline

Barre's residential values are suppressed by the decline of the granite industry. Once, granite quarries and mills employed 800+ people; now fewer than 150 jobs remain. This employment collapse has depressed residential demand.

A 1950s ranch in Barre (1,600 SF, 3 bed/1.5 bath) assessed at $265,000 is overvalued. Comparable sales in similar neighborhoods are $230–$250K. Listers assume granite industry stability that no longer exists.

Moreover, many residential properties in Barre sit adjacent to dormant or abandoned quarries and mills. These industrial scars depress residential value by 10–20%. Assessments have not incorporated this negative externality adequately.

**Example:** A home assessed at $280K three blocks from an abandoned granite quarry with standing water and industrial debris should be assessed at $235–$250K, not $280K. The quarry proximity is a material negative that reduces value.

### Waterbury: Flood Risk and Tourism Volatility

Waterbury has experienced catastrophic flooding (2011 Irene, 2017 corridor flood). Properties in flood zones or adjacent flood-prone areas are now valued with flood insurance premiums and buyout risk embedded. Yet assessments from 2022–2023 may not fully account for flood risk that materialized post-assessment.

Additionally, Waterbury's economy depends heavily on Cold Hollow Cider Mill and tourist traffic. A down tourism year (e.g., poor foliage season, pandemic, recession) directly impacts property values. Assessments assume stable tourism; market reality is volatile.

Residential properties in flood-adjacent neighborhoods (Mad River vicinity, Route 100 areas) assessed at $380–$420K should be assessed 8–15% lower to account for flood risk, flood insurance costs, and the psychological discount buyers apply to flood-prone areas.

### Plainfield and Rural Washington: Agricultural Decline

Plainfield and other rural Washington towns are experiencing a slow decline in owner-occupied agricultural properties. Farms are consolidating into larger operations or going dormant. A 50-acre property with a 1980s farmhouse and working barn may be assessed at $450K based on cost approach (land + structures), but its actual market value—as a residential property with agricultural legacy, not as a working farm—is $350–$380K.

Listers apply agricultural use values to properties that are no longer farmed, inflating assessments.

## Commercial Property Overtaxation in Washington County

### Barre: Granite Industry Industrial Properties

Barre's commercial base consists largely of granite-industry-related properties: quarries, cutting mills, fabrication shops, warehouses. These properties have experienced dramatic value collapses that assessments have not tracked.

A granite cutting mill (12,000 SF, multi-bay, commercial-industrial) assessed at $380,000 based on 2021 valuations is now worth $220–$250K. Why?

- Rock of Ages (largest granite employer in Vermont) cut production by 60% since 2015
- Granite fabrication is a low-margin business; shops operate at 4–6% NOI
- A 12,000 SF fabrication plant generating $180K gross revenue and $12K NOI (6.7% cap) would value at ~$180K, not $380K

Listers assessed these properties on equipment value and replacement cost, not actual income potential. Income approach yields 50–60% lower values.

**Example income analysis:**
- Granite mill actual gross revenue: $180,000/year
- Operating expenses (utilities, supplies, labor allocated to this location): $168,000
- NOI: $12,000
- Market cap rate for industrial: 8–9% (high risk)
- True market value: $12,000 ÷ 0.085 = $141,176
- Assessed value: $380,000
- Overvaluation: $238,824 (63%)

### Montpelier: Downtown Office and Retail

Montpelier's downtown has experienced slow economic decline. State government downsizing has reduced office demand; retail has declined as online shopping replaced Main Street shopping.

A downtown Montpelier office building (8,000 SF, 4-story) assessed at $520,000 is overvalued. Current occupancy is 65–75%; assumed occupancy is 90%+. Current rental rates are $12–$14/SF; assessed rates are $16–$18/SF (based on pre-pandemic data).

**Actual income:**
- Occupied space: 8,000 × 70% × $13/SF = $72,800
- Operating expenses: $19,000
- NOI: $53,800
- Cap rate: 7.5% (modest risk, downtown location)
- True value: $717,333

Wait—that suggests assessed value of $520K is actually low if we back-solve. The issue is the Lister's assessed value itself is based on incorrect assumptions. If Lister assumed $720K value on a 7% cap and $50.4K NOI, but actual NOI is $53.8K, the Lister's assumption of rent/occupancy that yields $50.4K NOI is the error. Let me recalculate.

If $520K assessed value implies 7% cap, Lister assumed $36,400 NOI. But actual NOI is $53,800. This suggests Lister severely underestimated income or used cost approach. Either way, the property isn't overvalued on income.

The issue in Montpelier is that Listers use **cost approach** for many downtown buildings (land + replacement cost − depreciation), not income approach. A building that cost $1.2M to build 30 years ago is assessed at $950K (after depreciation), but actually generates only $35K NOI (worth $500K at 7% cap). **Cost approach systematically overvalues older buildings.**

### Waterbury: Cold Hollow Cider Mill and Tourism Dependent

Waterbury's commercial base centers on Cold Hollow Cider Mill (largest regional employer/tourist draw) and tourism-related hospitality. Properties assessed on tourism occupancy assumptions face volatility.

A small hotel (20 rooms) assessed at $1.2M on 80% occupancy at $120/night:
- Assessed NOI: 20 × 80% × $120 × 365 = $876,000/year
- Actual NOI (60% occupancy, $110/night, minus expenses): $520,000/year
- Cap rate: 7.5%
- Assessed value: $1.2M (implied from NOI)
- True value: $693,333
- Overvaluation: $506,667 (42%)

### Route 2 Corridor: Commercial Strip Overvaluation

Route 2 (US-2) cuts through Washington County (Waterbury, Montpelier, Orange) and carries significant commercial traffic. Properties fronting Route 2 are assessed as if they're high-traffic commercial. But Route 2 is a pass-through highway, not a dense commercial corridor. Occupancy is typically 60–75%, not 85–90%.

A Route 2 commercial building (3,000 SF, multi-tenant) assessed at $280,000 on high-traffic assumptions should be reassessed at $220–$240K.

## The Vermont Grievance Process for Washington County Properties

### Grand List (April 1) → Lister Grievance (May 1)

Vermont law requires the Grand List to be filed by April 1. You have 30 days to file a written grievance.

**File with:**
- Montpelier: City Assessor, (802) 223-3271
- Barre: Town Listers, (802) 476-0218
- Waterbury: Town Listers, (802) 244-8152
- Plainfield: Town Listers, (802) 454-8550

**Deadline:** May 1 (approximately; confirm with your town)

**What to include:**
- Property address and ID number
- Assessed value
- Requested value
- Brief reason (overvalued based on comps, income analysis, market conditions)
- Any supporting evidence (sales printouts, appraisal, income statements)

### Lister Hearing (June)

Listers schedule hearings by mid-June. Attend and present:
- Comparable sales analysis (residential) or income analysis (commercial)
- Property condition report
- Market data or appraisal

Listers decide by late June.

### BCA Appeal (14 days after lister decision)

If you disagree, appeal to the Board of Civil Authority (your town's selectboard + voting members) within 14 days. File with the town clerk.

BCA will hold a hearing and conduct site inspection. Success rate: 5–12% reductions when evidence is solid.

### State Appeal (30 days after BCA decision)

Appeal to the Director of Property Valuation and Review if unsatisfied with BCA decision. Director reviews de novo. Success rate: 40–50% with strong evidence.

## Evidence That Wins Grievances in Washington County

### Residential: Comps with Market Adjustment

Washington County residential markets are smaller and slower-moving than Chittenden County. Finding 3–5 comparable sales within 12 months can be challenging. Use MLS data, deed transfers (public record at town clerk), and municipal property tax cards.

**Example: Montpelier colonial (1970s, 2,100 SF, 3 bed/2 bath, assessed $345K)**

Comps:
1. North Street, similar colonial, sold Jan 2026 for $318K
2. Elm Street, similar vintage but 4-bedroom, sold Mar 2026 for $365K; adjust –8% for extra bedroom = $335,800
3. Seminary Hill, 1965 ranch (older), sold Feb 2026 for $295K; adjust +6% for vintage = $312,700

Average: $322K

Assessment: $345K is 7% high. Request reduction to $322K.

### Commercial: Income Analysis with Actual Lease Data

For commercial property, gather:
- Actual rent roll with lease terms
- Operating expenses (verified by tax records, utility bills, insurance statements)
- NOI calculation
- Comparable cap rates from commercial brokers or CoStar

**Example: Barre granite mill**

Your property generates $180K gross revenue. Expenses are $168K. NOI: $12K.

Market cap rate for industrial: 8–9%.

True value: $12K ÷ 0.085 = $141K

If assessed at $380K, you have clear overvaluation.

### Granite Industry Decline Narrative

For Barre properties tied to granite, document:
- Decline in Rock of Ages employment (public information from company and Vermont Department of Labor)
- Decline in granite sales nationally (US Geological Survey data)
- Reduced activity at quarries and mills (photographs, permit records)
- Comparables showing depreciation in industrial property values

This narrative context helps BCA and the state understand why income has declined and justifies lower cap rates.

### Waterbury Flood Risk Documentation

For flood-adjacent properties, document:
- FEMA flood zone designation
- Flood insurance requirements (from lender or insurance agent)
- Historical floods affecting the property (2011 Irene, 2017 floods, damage records)
- Flood insurance premium costs
- Recent comps showing flood risk discount

Flood-adjacent properties sell at 8–15% discounts. Assessments must reflect this discount.

6. **Cost Approach Errors**: Montpelier and Barre have many older commercial buildings assessed on cost approach (replacement cost minus depreciation), not income approach. This systematically overvalues low-income properties.
