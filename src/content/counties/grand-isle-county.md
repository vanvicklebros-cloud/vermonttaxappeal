## Why Commercial Properties Are Overvalued in Grand Isle County

### Waterfront Hospitality & Recreational Overvaluation

Waterfront hotels, marinas, and recreational businesses are systematically overvalued using inflated occupancy and revenue assumptions:

**The error:**
1. **Peak-season revenue annualized** – Listers use summer peak (June–August) revenues as annualized basis, ignoring spring/fall shoulder and winter slowness
2. **Inflated occupancy rates** – Assuming 70%+ occupancy for seasonal waterfront properties when actual data shows 50–65%
3. **Inappropriate cap rates** – Using 5% cap rates (urban stabilized hospitality) instead of 8–10% for remote, seasonal, weather-dependent waterfront properties
4. **Ignored environmental seasonality** – Winter ice/wind makes properties unsuitable for occupancy; Listers assess "as if" year-round usable

**Real-world example:** A 30-room waterfront resort:
- **Lister calculation:** $1.1M × 45% NOI ÷ 5% cap = $9.9M
- **Market reality:** $650K × 45% NOI ÷ 8.5% cap = $3.44M
- **Overvaluation:** $6.46M (188% above market)

### Marina & Waterfront Recreation Facility Overvaluation

Marinas and boat rental facilities are overvalued using peak-season capacity assumptions:

- **Peak-season revenue annualized** – Summer boating season (June–August) revenues annualized without accounting for shoulder/off-season decline
- **Unrealistic slip rates** – Assuming properties maintain peak summer rental rates year-round
- **Ignored weather/seasonal restrictions** – Lake ice (November–April) makes properties unusable; Listers ignore this constraint
- **Maintenance & cap ex ignored** – Waterfront properties have substantial seasonal maintenance; ignored in income valuation

### Agritourism & Farm Stand Overvaluation

Agricultural properties with agritourism components (orchards, vineyards, farm stands, agritourism venues) are overvalued:

- **Peak-season revenue annualized** – Fall foliage/harvest season (Sept–Oct) revenues treated as annual baseline
- **Assumed scaling of agritourism** – Listers assume farms will continuously expand agritourism revenue; actual growth is limited
- **Commodity price assumptions** – Using prior years' high apple/vineyard prices rather than current market rates
- **Expense under-allocation** – Labor, equipment, and infrastructure costs for agritourism ignored or understated

## The Vermont Grievance & Appeal Process: Your Pathway to a Reduction

Vermont law (32 V.S.A. § 4111) guarantees all property owners the right to challenge Grand List assessments. Here's the exact timeline for Grand Isle County:

### Step 1: The Grand List Filing (April 1)

Your town Listers file the completed Grand List by April 1. You're entitled to inspect it at your town office.

### Step 2: Grievance Filing (30 days from Grand List)

Within 30 days of Grand List filing, file a **written grievance** with your town Listers. Grand Isle town deadlines vary:
- **South Hero:** May 5 (2026)
- **North Hero:** May 7 (2026)
- **Grand Isle:** May 6 (2026)
- **Alburgh:** May 8 (2026)

**Your grievance must include:**
- Property identification (Grand List number, street address)
- Statement of alleged valuation or classification error
- Supporting evidence (comps, condition reports, income documentation)
- Requested assessed value

**We prepare and file your grievance.** You provide property details and supporting evidence.

### Step 3: Listers' Hearing (60–90 days after grievance)

Town Listers schedule a formal hearing. Your opportunity to present evidence; Listers must provide written findings.

### Step 4: Board of Civil Authority (BCA) Appeal (14 days after denial)

You have **14 days** to appeal to your town's **Board of Civil Authority** (select board).

**BCA standard:** You must prove by clear and convincing evidence that Listers' valuation is wrong.

### Step 5: Director of Property Valuation and Review (State Appeal)

If BCA denies your appeal, appeal to Vermont's **Director** (state level).

**State appeal standard:** De novo review; Director weighs evidence independently.

## Winning Grievance Evidence: What Persuades Listers & the BCA

### Comparable Sales Analysis (Most Powerful)

**Residential properties:** Find 3–5 recent arm's-length sales of similar properties in the same neighborhood, sold within 12 months. Adjust for square footage, age, condition, lot size, waterfront status, views, renovation recency.

**Grand Isle opportunity:** Waterfront properties are selling at 15–25% discounts from listed prices. Document listing-to-sale ratios; a property listed at $520K but sold at $420K directly contradicts a $480K assessment.

**Commercial properties:** Recent sales of similar waterfront hospitality, marina, or agritourism properties are decisive. Any recent sale is powerful evidence in Grand Isle's limited transaction environment.

### Income and Expense Documentation

**For waterfront hospitality/recreational properties:**
- 2–3 years of actual tax returns (Schedule C, Form 1065)
- Detailed P&L statements (gross revenue, operating expenses, NOI)
- Bank statements documenting actual revenue
- Payroll records showing actual labor costs
- Utility bills showing seasonal variation and off-season costs

**For seasonal properties:**
- Actual booking data showing occupancy rates (critical if <65%)
- Documented revenue by month (prove June–August ≠ annual average)
- Deferred maintenance lists and contractor estimates
- Management company agreements showing actual fees

**For agritourism/farm operations:**
- 3–5 years of tax returns showing actual revenue trends
- Commodity price data showing declines from prior years
- Documentation of seasonal/peak revenue patterns
- Agricultural use documentation (orchard yields, vineyard production, crop records)

### Professional Appraisals

A certified MAI appraisal (cost: $1,500–$3,500) carries tremendous weight. Ensure the appraiser has experience with waterfront, seasonal, and agricultural property valuations in Vermont.

### Condition and Physical Deficiency Reports

Document deferred maintenance:
- Roof age and replacement cost
- HVAC, plumbing, electrical issues
- Foundation or structural damage
- Water damage, moisture intrusion, ice damage
- Waterfront erosion, seawall/dock condition, dredging needs
- Contractor quotes for necessary repairs

Example: A $400K waterfront property with $70K dock repair + $45K erosion control + $30K water damage = $255K market value, not $400K.

### Agricultural Property Classification

For properties eligible for Current Use Program (CPR):
- Proof that property qualifies for Current Use assessment
- Documentation of agricultural operations (orchard data, vineyard yields, crop records)
- Proof that Listers misclassified as residential or agritourism-only
