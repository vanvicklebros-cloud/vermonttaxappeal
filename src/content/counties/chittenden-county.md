## Chittenden County: Vermont's Most Overvalued Real Estate Market

Chittenden County is Vermont's economic and real estate powerhouse. The region stretches from Burlington (population 45,000) through South Burlington, Colchester, Essex, Williston, Shelburne, and Winooski, with combined population over 170,000 and a Grand List exceeding $27 billion. Median property tax bills range from $5,100 (Williston) to $6,800 (Burlington), totaling over $540 million annually across the county.

Yet this prosperity masks a profound tax assessment crisis. As real estate growth has cooled post-2022 and market fundamentals have deteriorated, property tax assessments—locked in at 2023–2024 valuations—have become dramatically disconnected from current market reality. Residential homeowners in neighborhoods like Burlington's Old North End, South Burlington's desirable Sycamore Street corridor, and Williston's new construction are being taxed on 2023 peak values in a 2026 market with tighter credit, higher rates, longer selling times, and measurable price declines.

Commercial property suffers even more. Retail properties anchored by national chains face structural headwinds. Office space haunted by hybrid work sits half-empty. Hospitality recovers to normalized levels, not boom levels. Yet assessment rolls haven't caught up.

## Residential Overtaxation in Chittenden County

### The 2025 Reappraisal Trap

Burlington completed a comprehensive reappraisal in 2025, using 2023–2024 sales transactions as comparables. This methodology created a structural overvaluation problem. Sales in late 2023 and early 2024 occurred when 30-year mortgage rates were 6.5–7%, inventory was tight, and bidding wars were common. Today, with rates at 7.5–8.2%, inventory up 35%, and average market time at 75+ days, those 2023 transactions no longer represent current fair market value.

**Example: Burlington Old North End colonial (3 bed, 2 bath, 1,850 SF, built 1968)**
- Assessed at: $465,000 (based on 2023 comp that sold for $485,000)
- Recent comparable sales (Feb–Mar 2026): $405K, $410K, $425K (average: $413K)
- Overvaluation: $52,000 (11.2%)
- Tax overpayment (assuming 1.3% rate): $676/year

The Lister used one high comp; market reality shows 10–15% depreciation since reappraisal.

### South Burlington: Retail-Proximity Inflation

South Burlington's residential valuations are inflated by proximity to Route 7 retail and employment centers. Listers apply a "commercial spillover" uplift to properties within 0.5 miles of shopping malls and office parks, assuming higher values. In reality, proximity to Route 7 is a **negative** for residential—noise, traffic, pollution, less desirable neighborhood character.

A 3-bedroom colonial on Shelburne Road (south of the commercial district) assessed at $485,000 in 2025 is overvalued by 12–15%. True comps in that micromarket are $415–$445K. The Lister applied a +8% commercial proximity uplift that shouldn't exist.

### Colchester and Williston: New Construction Overbuilding

Both towns have experienced rapid new construction since 2021 (200+ new homes in each). Listers assume new construction has not depreciated, applying cost approach valuations (land + construction cost − minimal depreciation). But new construction in these towns faces:
- Slower sales velocity (45–60 days vs. 30–45 in 2021)
- 8–12% price reductions from builders to move inventory
- Lengthening absorption rates (oversupply in subdivisions)

A 2023 new home assessed at $520,000 (cost approach) now sells for $445,000 (market approach). The assessment is 17% high.

### Multi-Family Residential: Income Approach Errors

Two-family and three-family homes in Burlington, Colchester, and South Burlington are valued using the income approach—treating them as rental investments. Listers assume:
- 8% cap rate
- 100% occupancy
- Rent at market rates

Reality (2026):
- Cap rates are 6.5–7.5% (riskier properties with turnover)
- Occupancy in Northeast market is 92–94%
- Rents have plateaued; some markets showing softness
- Turnover costs (painting, repairs, vacancy between tenants) consume 15–20% of gross rent

A 3-unit building generating $36,000/year gross rent (3 × $1,000/month) is valued by Lister at $450,000 using 8% cap rate on $36K. True value:
- Vacancy + turnover loss: 8% of $36K = $2,880
- Effective gross income: $33,120
- Operating expenses (insurance, tax, maintenance): ~$8K
- NOI: $25,120
- Cap rate at 7%: $25,120 ÷ 0.07 = $358,857

Lister's valuation is $91K (25%) too high.

## Commercial Property Overtaxation in Chittenden County

### Retail Apocalypse: South Burlington and Essex Junction

The South Burlington retail corridor—historically the region's commercial heart—is in structural decline:
- Bed Bath & Beyond closed (2023)
- Sears closed (2021)
- Pier 1 closed (2020)
- Anchor tenants at South Burlington Plaza pay 40–50% below market rent
- Small-shop vacancy rates are 15–25%

Yet listers value these properties using 2019–2021 fundamentals, assuming strong anchor tenancy and 95%+ occupancy. An 8,000 SF strip center with one anchor at $8/SF and 30% vacancy in smaller spaces is valued as if it has 90% occupancy at $14/SF.

**Actual financial analysis:**
- Anchor tenant: 2,500 SF at $8/SF = $20,000
- Small shops: 5,500 SF, 40% vacant, average $12/SF on occupied = $39,600
- Gross rent: $59,600
- Vacancy + collection loss: 15% = $8,940
- Effective income: $50,660
- Operating expenses (property tax, insurance, CAM, maintenance): $18,000
- NOI: $32,660
- Market cap rate: 7.5% (reflecting risk)
- Market value: $435,467

Lister's valuation: $580,000 (based on 6.5% cap rate and 95% occupancy assumption).
Overvaluation: $144,533 (33%).

### Office Space: Hybrid Work Reality Not Priced In

Multi-tenant office buildings around downtown Burlington, Essex Junction, and the Route 2 corridor are overvalued. Hybrid work adoption means:
- 20–30% reduced space demand
- Sublease rates 10–15% below asking
- Build-to-suit concessions (free rent months, tenant improvement allowances)
- Cap rates have risen to 6.5–7.5% (from 5.5% pre-2021)

A Class B office building (8,000 SF, 3-story) with 75% occupancy at $15/SF, where listers assume 95% occupancy at $18/SF:
- Actual gross rent: 8,000 × 0.75 × $15 = $90,000
- Lister's assumed: 8,000 × 0.95 × $18 = $136,800
- Operating expenses: $22,000
- Actual NOI: $68,000
- Lister's assumed NOI: $114,800
- Market cap rate: 7%
- True value: $971,428
- Lister's valuation: $1,640,000
- Overvaluation: $668,572 (69%)

### Hospitality: Post-Pandemic Crash

Hotels and inns in Burlington, Colchester, and Williston saw occupancy spikes during pandemic (80–85%) as demand for Vermont tourism surged. Assessments were locked in at 85% occupancy. Current reality:
- Summer occupancy: 70–75%
- Shoulder seasons: 55–65%
- Winter (non-ski): 45–50%
- Annual blended: 62–65%

A 40-room hotel assessed on 85% occupancy at $120/night average:
- Assessed NOI basis: 40 × 85% × $120 × 365 = $1,489,200
- Actual NOI basis: 40 × 65% × $110 × 365 = $1,039,000
- Cap rate: 7% (hospitality risk)
- Assessed value: $1,489,200 ÷ 0.07 = $21.27M
- True value: $1,039,000 ÷ 0.07 = $14.84M
- Overvaluation: $6.43M (43%)

### Waterfront Land Inflation

Lake Champlain waterfront properties (Shelburne, Charlotte, Colchester) benefit from genuine premium. But Listers often apply 40–50% waterfront uplift to properties that are technically "waterfront" on zoning maps but 300–500 feet from actual water access, hidden by vegetation, or with no utility easement.

True waterfront with direct lake access and buildable: 40% premium justified.
Nearby but not directly accessible: 5–10% premium at most.

Yet assessments apply uniform uplift. A 0.75-acre parcel 400 feet from the lake assessed at $180K (with 35% waterfront uplift) should be assessed at $135K without the uplift.

## The Vermont Grievance Process: Step-by-Step for Chittenden County

### Phase 1: Grand List to Lister Grievance (April 1 → May 1)

Vermont law (32 V.S.A. § 4111) requires the Grand List to be filed by April 1. You have 30 days to grieve. **File in writing.**

**Deadline**: May 1 (confirm with your specific town—some allow until May 15)

**File with**: Town Lister (Colchester, Williston, Winooski, Essex) or City Assessor (Burlington, South Burlington, Winooski)

**Required elements:**
- Property address and ID number
- Assessed value (from Grand List)
- Reason: "Property overvalued" or specific claim (too high based on comps, income analysis, condition)
- Requested value with brief justification
- Your signature and contact info

**Example grievance letter:**

> [Your Name]
> [Address]
> 
> May 1, 2026
> 
> Burlington Assessor's Office
> 
> **GRIEVANCE OF PROPERTY VALUATION**
> 
> Property: [Your address], PIN [####]
> 
> Assessed Value (2026 Grand List): $465,000
> 
> Requested Value: $415,000
> 
> Reason: Overvaluation. Recent comparable sales (Feb–Mar 2026) in Old North End show properties of similar vintage and condition selling for $405–$425K. Assessed value does not reflect current market conditions.
> 
> Respectfully,
> [Your signature]

**Lister's duty**: Listers must review your grievance and schedule a hearing by mid-June.

### Phase 2: Lister Hearing (June)

Attend if possible. Present:
- Comparable sales printouts (3–5 recent sales, similar properties)
- Property condition photos (roof age, HVAC condition, deferred maintenance)
- Income data (if applicable)
- Any appraisal or assessment opinion

Listers will explain their methodology. Ask questions. The goal is to build a record of your evidence.

Lister's decision typically arrives in late June.

### Phase 3: BCA Appeal (14 days after lister decision)

If you disagree with lister, appeal to the Board of Civil Authority within 14 days. **This deadline is absolute.** Miss it and you lose your appeal right for that tax year.

File with: Town Clerk or City Clerk

**BCA hearing** typically occurs in July–August. The BCA is your town's selectboard plus citizen members. They will:
1. Review lister's evidence and your evidence
2. Hold a hearing (you present, lister presents, BCA questions)
3. **Conduct a physical site inspection** of your property (mandatory)
4. Vote and issue a decision

BCA decisions are typically 5–12% reductions when evidence is solid.

### Phase 4: State Appeal (30 days after BCA decision)

If BCA denies full relief, appeal to the **Director of Property Valuation and Review** within 30 days. State-level appeal is de novo (no deference to BCA). The Director's office applies stricter legal standards but is sophisticated in appraisal methodology.

Success rate at state level: 40–50% with strong evidence.

## Evidence That Wins Grievances in Chittenden County

### Residential: Comparable Sales Analysis

The gold standard is **recent arms-length sales of comparable properties.**

**Criteria for "comparable":**
- Same town or adjacent neighborhood
- Sold within 12 months (ideally 6 months)
- Similar age (±10 years)
- Similar size (±200 SF)
- Similar condition
- Similar features (garage, basement, lot size)

**Adjustment factors:**
- Age: 1–2% per 5 years older
- Condition: 5–15% for major issues (old roof, HVAC, foundation issues)
- Size: 1% per 50 SF difference
- Features: 3–8% per major feature (garage, deck, extra bathroom)

**Example winning analysis:**

Your property:
- Burlington, Old North End
- 1968 colonial
- 3 bed, 2 bath
- 1,850 SF
- 0.25-acre lot
- Assessed: $465,000

Comparable sales:
1. **Comp A**: Sylvester Street, 1971 colonial, 1,900 SF, 3 bed/2 bath, sold Feb 2026 for $425,000
   - Adjustment: +2% for newer (+1% younger) + 2% size difference = +3%
   - Adjusted value: $438,000

2. **Comp B**: North Street, 1965 colonial, 1,800 SF, 3 bed/2 bath (older roof), sold Jan 2026 for $410,000
   - Adjustment: –2% (older) + 1% (roof age depreciation shows in market price already) = –1%
   - Adjusted value: $405,900

3. **Comp C**: North Prospect, 1967 ranch, 1,750 SF, 3 bed/1.5 bath (fewer baths), sold Mar 2026 for $418,000
   - Adjustment: +3% size + 8% for missing half bath = +11%
   - Adjusted value: $464,000 (overstating due to style difference; lower weight to this comp)

**Indicated value range**: $405K–$438K (average ~$420K)

Your assessment of $465K is 10–14% high.

**Request reduction to $420K**. BCA typically grants 5–10% reduction when you present three solid comps.

### Commercial: Income Analysis with Actual Data

For income-producing property, present:
- Actual rent roll (tenant names, square footage, rent amount, lease expiry)
- Lease agreements (proving rent and terms)
- Actual operating expenses (property tax, insurance, CAM, maintenance)
- Actual NOI
- Comparable cap rates (from CoStar, CCIM network, or commercial broker opinion)

**Example:**

Your 6,000 SF office building assessed at $900,000.

**Your actual income:**
- Tenant A: 2,000 SF at $16/SF = $32,000/year (lease expires 2027)
- Tenant B: 2,500 SF at $14/SF = $35,000/year (lease expires 2028)
- Tenant C: 1,000 SF at $12/SF (newly vacant, 3 months) = $12,000/year effective
- Tenant D: 500 SF at $10/SF (older tenant, month-to-month) = $5,000/year
- **Gross rent**: $84,000

**Actual operating expenses:**
- Property tax: $12,000
- Insurance: $3,500
- Common area maintenance: $2,000
- Repairs and maintenance: $4,000
- Utilities (landlord pays): $1,500
- Total: $23,000

**Actual NOI**: $84,000 – $23,000 = $61,000

**Market cap rate**: 7% (per CoStar, comparing similar multi-tenant office buildings in Burlington)

**True value**: $61,000 ÷ 0.07 = $871,429

**Lister's assessment**: $900,000

**Overvaluation**: Only $28,571 (3%). This is a case where the assessment is reasonable, or you need deeper analysis.

But if Lister's $900K was based on assumed NOI of $75K (implying tenant mix and occupancy assumptions that don't match reality), you have a 13% overvaluation case.

### Third-Party Appraisal

An MAI appraisal (Appraisal Institute member) costs $3,000–$6,000 but carries weight at BCA and state level. Appraisers follow USPAP and independently reconcile value indicators. 

If appraisal supports $420K and you're assessed at $465K, the appraiser's opinion is persuasive to the BCA.

## Market Context: Why Chittenden County Grievances Succeed

Several structural factors make Chittenden County grievances highly successful:

1. **Post-Boom Market Correction**: The 2021–2022 real estate surge is history. Tight credit, higher rates, and inventory are all working to lower prices 10–15% from peak. Assessments locked in at peak are provably wrong.

2. **Retail Structural Decline**: South Burlington's retail dominance is fading. Mall traffic, anchor occupancy, and retail rents are all declining. Commercial property owners have strong evidence.

3. **Office Hybrid Reality**: Every commercial office lister in the county is dealing with hybrid work adjustments. BCA members understand that 95% occupancy assumptions are outdated.

4. **Hospitality Normalization**: Post-pandemic occupancy is settling to sustainable levels. Assessments must adjust accordingly.

5. **Waterfront Land Volatility**: Year-round resident saturation around Lake Champlain has reduced demand. Land values in secondary waterfront locations have softened.

6. **UVM and State Budget Constraints**: UVM's modest payroll growth and Vermont state budget pressures mean wage growth (historically strong) is not supporting residential demand growth. Price appreciation is stalling.
