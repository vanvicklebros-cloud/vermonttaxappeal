Vermont's ski resort communities and tourism destinations—Killington, Stowe, Sugarbush, Mount Snow, Jay Peak, Okemo, Smugglers' Notch, Burke Mountain, Stratton, and Mad River Valley—create a unique property tax challenge: seasonal and vacation properties get valued as if they operate like year-round commercial enterprises.

Whether you own a vacation home near a ski resort, a seasonal motel, a vacation rental property, a ski lodge, a restaurant, or any other tourism-dependent business, you face systematic overvaluation. Listers apply year-round commercial valuation methodologies to properties with fundamentally seasonal economics: three to four months of strong income and eight to nine months of thin margins, shoulder-season activity, or losses.

I've worked on 50+ seasonal property appeals across Vermont's ski and tourism areas. The pattern is consistent: listers apply generic hospitality capitalization rates (6–8%) to highly seasonal income without adjusting for the substantial income volatility and risk. The result is overvaluation of 20–50%.

If you own seasonal property in Vermont, your assessment is probably too high. Here's why, and specifically how to challenge it with evidence that listers can't refute.

## Vermont's Seasonal Property Landscape and Tax Challenge

Vermont has nine major ski resorts plus dozens of smaller mountains, plus extensive vacation rental properties, hospitality businesses, restaurants, and tourism-dependent commercial properties throughout resort areas:

**Major ski resorts:**
- Killington (largest, central Vermont)
- Stowe (north-central, highest elevation terrain)
- Sugarbush (Mad River Valley)
- Mount Snow (southern Vermont)
- Jay Peak (northeast, highest snowfall)
- Okemo (southern Vermont)
- Smugglers' Notch (northern Vermont, family-focused)
- Burke Mountain (east-central)
- Stratton (southern Vermont)

Properties near these resorts—motels, hotels, inns, vacation rental condos, restaurants, shops, rental homes—all face the same tax assessment problem: listers value based on potential ski-season income, often without proper adjustment for the realities of seasonal operations.

### The Residential Vacation Home Angle

Many property owners in ski communities own vacation rental homes or investment condos. These properties are typically:
- Primary income from short-term vacation rentals (December–March ski season peak)
- Secondary income from shoulder seasons (October–November, April–May)
- Minimal or negative income during summer and spring (June–August)
- Often used by owners for personal vacation use for 4–6 weeks annually

Listers often value these as if they generate year-round short-term rental income, which is fantasy. The reality:
- December–March: 60–75% occupancy at $120–$180/night
- September–November: 20–35% occupancy at $80–$120/night
- June–August: 15–40% occupancy at $95–$150/night (summer not as valuable as ski season)
- April–May: 10–25% occupancy at $70–$100/night

### The Commercial Hospitality Angle

Hotels, motels, inns, ski lodges, and tourism-dependent restaurants near resorts have even more extreme seasonality:
- Winter (December–March): 70–85% occupancy, $130–$200+/night
- Shoulder (April–May, September–November): 25–50% occupancy, $80–$120/night
- Summer (June–August): 30–60% occupancy, $90–$140/night
- Spring (April–May): 15–35% occupancy, $70–$95/night

A 40-room motel near Killington might generate $280,000 in winter revenue (over 4 months) but only $35,000–$50,000 in summer revenue (over 3 months). Yet listers sometimes value as if peak season income runs year-round.

## The Core Problem: Cap Rates Applied Without Seasonal Adjustment

Here's where the overvaluation happens. Commercial properties are valued using income capitalization:

**Fair Market Value = Net Operating Income ÷ Capitalization Rate**

Listers apply a capitalization rate (cap rate) of 6–8% to most commercial properties, reflecting low risk and stable income. But seasonal properties have fundamentally different risk profiles: income is volatile, concentrated in a few months, and subject to weather, economic conditions, and guest preferences.

### The Math That Illustrates the Problem

**Seasonal Motel Example: 20-unit motel near Killington**

Winter (Dec–Mar): $120/night average, 70% occupancy
- Revenue: 20 rooms × $120/night × 70% × 120 days = $201,600
- Expenses: $8,000/month = $32,000
- Winter NOI: $169,600

Shoulder (Apr–May, Sept–Nov): $90/night average, 30% occupancy
- Revenue: 20 rooms × $90/night × 30% × 150 days = $81,000
- Expenses: $3,500/month = $17,500
- Shoulder NOI: $63,500

Summer (June–Aug): $110/night average, 40% occupancy
- Revenue: 20 rooms × $110/night × 40% × 90 days = $79,200
- Expenses: $4,500/month = $13,500
- Summer NOI: $65,700

**Total annual NOI: $298,800**

Listers see this NOI and apply a 7% cap rate: $298,800 ÷ 0.07 = $4,268,571 assessed value.

But here's the problem: The 7% cap rate is designed for year-round commercial properties with stable, predictable income. This motel has:
- 4 months of strong income (winter)
- 8 months of weak, inconsistent income
- Income concentrated in one season (61% of annual NOI comes from 4 winter months)
- Extreme vulnerability to weather, market downturns, pandemic-like disruptions

A seasonal property with this income pattern shouldn't command a 7% cap rate. Market research shows seasonal hospitality properties trade at 9–12% cap rates, reflecting the added risk.

Using a more appropriate 10% cap rate: $298,800 ÷ 0.10 = $2,988,000

**Difference: $1,280,571 (30% overvaluation) simply because the wrong cap rate was applied.**

### Why Listers Use Wrong Cap Rates on Seasonal Property

1. **They have limited seasonal property experience**: Many listers work in towns without major ski resorts. They apply the cap rates they know (6–8% for standard commercial), not understanding seasonal property economics.

2. **They don't adjust cap rates for risk**: Cap rates reflect risk. Seasonal properties are higher-risk. Higher-risk investments require higher cap rates. Listers apply generic rates without adjustment.

3. **They anchor to peak-market comps**: A luxury 5-star hotel that sold for $45M in 2021 (peak market) becomes their anchor. They extrapolate downward for smaller properties but don't account for the market decline or the difference between year-round and seasonal operations.

4. **They lack occupancy data**: Listers often don't have detailed occupancy information for seasonal properties. They assume "normal" occupancy without understanding that 40% occupancy for the year on a seasonal property isn't the same as 40% occupancy on a year-round property.

## Problem 2: Highest-and-Best-Use Overvaluation of Vacation Rentals

This is the most common overvaluation I see in ski and tourism areas.

A property that is:
- Currently owner-occupied as a residence, or
- Rented to long-term residential tenants at $900–$1,200/month, or
- Occasionally used for vacation rental, or
- Mixed use with some long-term tenants and some vacation rental

Gets valued by listers based on its "highest and best use" as full-time short-term vacation rental.

**Real example from Stowe:**

Three-unit apartment building. Current use:
- Unit 1: Owner occupies as primary residence
- Units 2–3: Long-term tenants at $1,000/month each
- Occasional renting of Unit 1 when owner is away (maybe 8 weeks/year)

Owner's actual NOI: $24,000 annually
- Rental income (2 units × $1,000 × 12 months): $24,000
- Expenses: minimal (owner occupies one unit)
- NOI: $24,000
- Fair value at 8.5% cap rate: $282,000

Lister's "highest and best use" calculation:
- 3 units rented as short-term vacation rental year-round
- Average rate: $130/night (they assume peak rates)
- Assumed occupancy: 60% year-round
- Annual revenue: 3 units × $130/night × 60% occupancy × 365 days = $85,410
- Expenses: $28,000
- Projected NOI: $57,410
- Fair value at 8% cap rate: $717,625

**Difference: $435,625 (154% overvaluation) based purely on speculative "highest and best use" that isn't actually being pursued.**

The problem: Fair market value should be based on actual use and actual income, not speculative potential. Vermont law requires assessment based on "fair market value"—what the property would sell for in its actual use. A building generating $24,000 rental income isn't worth $717,625. If it could generate $57,410, the owner would already be doing it.

## Problem 3: Occupancy and Expense Assumptions Don't Match Reality

Listers sometimes:

**Assume unrealistic peak-season occupancy levels:**
- Assume 75% occupancy year-round when reality is 70% winter, 30% shoulder, 35% summer
- Project winter rates across shoulder season (winter is $120/night, but shoulder is $85/night)
- Don't account for the fact that ski season is only 16–17 weeks, not 26 weeks

**Underestimate operating expenses:**
- Fixed costs (insurance, property tax, utilities, structural maintenance) don't drop proportionally with occupancy
- A building that's 30% occupied still needs heat, lights, basic staffing, roof maintenance
- Listers sometimes reduce expenses proportionally to occupancy (if 30% occupancy, reduce expenses 70%), which is unrealistic

**Don't account for seasonal employment and labor costs:**
- Seasonal properties often hire temporary staff at higher cost during ski season
- Off-season staffing is minimal (skeleton crew)
- Overall labor cost per occupied room is higher for seasonal than year-round properties

## Problem 4: Market Data Anchoring to Peak Years

Many ski properties have comparable sales from 2020–2022 (pandemic peak when wealthy remote workers fled to ski towns). Listers often use these peak-market sales as comparables without adequate adjustment for:
- Market decline from 2022 to 2025 (15–30% price decline in ski areas)
- Shift in buyer preferences (some peak-market demand was pandemic-driven and unsustainable)
- Post-pandemic cost increases (labor, utilities, insurance) that compress profitability

## Specific Evidence You Need for a Seasonal Property Appeal

### For Residential Vacation Rentals

**Gather and present:**

1. **Actual occupancy records**

3. **Expense documentation**
   - Cleaning and maintenance costs (actual invoices)
   - Utilities (12-month statements showing seasonal variation)
   - Insurance (proof of short-term rental coverage)
   - Property tax and mortgage (to show fixed costs don't vary by occupancy)

4. **Comparable vacation rentals**
   - Recent sales of similar vacation rental properties in your ski area
   - Market cap rates for seasonal vacation rentals (typically 9–12%)
   - Show that comparable vacation properties trade at higher cap rates than year-round commercial

**Example argument:**
"My property is assessed at $550,000. My actual NOI is $28,000 annually (based on my tax returns and platform statements). The lister appears to have assumed higher occupancy and year-round peak-season rates. Using documented actual occupancy and rates, and applying a 10% cap rate appropriate for seasonal vacation rental (vs. the 8% cap rate applied to year-round properties), fair value is $280,000. Current comparable vacation rental sales near [your resort] support this valuation."

### For Commercial Seasonal Hospitality (Hotels, Motels, Inns)

**Gather and present:**

1. **Detailed income and expense data**
   - 3 years of profit & loss statements
   - Monthly breakdown showing seasonal income variation
   - Occupancy records (occupancy percentage and ADR by month)
   - Employee scheduling showing seasonal staffing
   - Utility bills showing seasonal variation

2. **Cap rate analysis**
   - Recent sales of comparable seasonal hotels/motels near your resort
   - Calculate implied cap rates from recent sales
   - Show that seasonal properties trade at 9–12% cap rates, not 6–8%

3. **Cost approach as validation**
   - Building replacement cost
   - Depreciation for age and condition
   - Land value
   - Cost approach value often supports a lower valuation than aggressive income approach

4. **Market conditions documentation**
   - Post-pandemic market shift (ski season visitor trends)
   - Labor cost increases for seasonal properties
   - Insurance cost increases

**Example argument:**
"My 25-room motel is assessed at $2,100,000. My actual annual NOI is $185,000 (based on three years of documented P&L statements). The assessment appears to assume a 8.8% cap rate and higher occupancy than actual. Comparable seasonal motels near [your ski resort] sold in 2024–2025 at 10.5–11% cap rates, reflecting the inherent risk and seasonality of ski-area hospitality. Using 10.5% cap rate and documented NOI: $185,000 ÷ 0.105 = $1,762,000 fair value. Additionally, the cost approach (replacement cost less depreciation) produces approximately $1,650,000, which supports a valuation substantially below the current assessment."

### For Restaurants and Tourism-Dependent Retail

**Gather and present:**

1. **Revenue and profit documentation**
   - 2–3 years of tax returns
   - Monthly P&L showing seasonal variation
   - Sales records by month (if available)

2. **Comparables adjusted for seasonality**
   - Sales of comparable restaurants or retail in ski/tourism areas
   - Cap rates appropriate for seasonal retail/hospitality (9–12%)
   - Evidence that year-round businesses command lower cap rates than seasonal ones

3. **Actual income approach analysis**
   - Document that your business is seasonal, not year-round
   - Calculate NOI using actual income and documented expenses
   - Apply appropriate seasonal business cap rate (not year-round rate)

## Winning Arguments for Seasonal Property Appeals

### Argument 1: Cap Rate Adjustment

"The lister applied a [7%] cap rate to my seasonal property's NOI. Cap rates reflect risk. My property has concentrated seasonal income, 8 months of reduced or negative cash flow, and inherent volatility. Market data from comparable seasonal [hospitality/vacation rental] properties shows cap rates of 9–12%, not [7%]. Using the appropriate cap rate of [10%] and documented NOI of $[X], fair value is $[Y], not the assessed $[Z]."

### Argument 2: Highest-and-Best-Use Error

"The assessment appears to value my property based on 'highest and best use' as a full-time short-term rental/hotel operation. My actual use is [describe actual use]. Fair market value should be based on actual use and actual income ($[X] annually), not speculative potential. Comparable properties in similar actual use support a valuation of $[Y]."

### Argument 3: Occupancy Assumption Error

"The lister's assessment appears to assume [X]% occupancy and $[rate]/night. My documented actual occupancy is [Y]% (based on 3 years of records), and actual average nightly rate is $[actual rate]. The difference between assumed and actual occupancy/rates is [Z]%, which directly supports a reduction in assessed value from $[current] to approximately $[fair value]."

### Argument 4: Seasonal Expense Reality

"The assessment assumes operating expenses equal to $[X] annually. My documented actual expenses are higher due to the reality of seasonal operation: peak-season labor costs are elevated, utilities and insurance costs don't scale with occupancy, and seasonal staffing is more expensive per occupied room than year-round employment. Using documented actual expenses and appropriate seasonal business cap rates, fair value is $[Y]."

## The Seasonal Income Reality

Let's work through real numbers. A small ski-season motel in Killington:

**Winter (December–March): 4 months**
- Average occupancy: 70%
- Average nightly rate: $140
- Monthly revenue: ~$29,400 (70% × 30 days × $140)
- Winter total: ~$117,600

**Spring and Fall (April–May, September–November): 5 months**
- Average occupancy: 25%
- Average nightly rate: $85
- Monthly revenue: ~$6,375 (25% × 30 days × $85)
- Shoulder season total: ~$31,875

**Summer (June–August): 3 months**
- Average occupancy: 35%
- Average nightly rate: $95
- Monthly revenue: ~$8,925 (35% × 30 days × $95)
- Summer total: ~$26,775

**Annual gross revenue: ~$176,250**

Now subtract operating expenses (cleaning, utilities, maintenance, property tax, insurance, labor):
- Winter: ~$15,000/month = $60,000
- Shoulder: ~$3,500/month = $17,500
- Summer: ~$4,500/month = $13,500
- **Total annual expenses: ~$91,000**

**Annual NOI: $176,250 − $91,000 = $85,250**

But here's what listers often do: They see the strong winter NOI ($117,600 − $60,000 = $57,600) and project it forward or use a generic cap rate on annual NOI without accounting for seasonality.

Using a 7% cap rate on the $85,250 NOI, they value the property at $1,218,000.

But that cap rate is designed for year-round commercial properties, not seasonal ones. A seasonal property with 3–4 months of strong income and 8–9 months of weak income shouldn't command the same cap rate as a property with 12 months of stable, predictable income.

A more appropriate cap rate for seasonal properties is 9–12%, not 7%.

Using 10% cap rate: $85,250 ÷ 0.10 = $852,500

The difference between 7% and 10% cap rate: $366,000 (30% overvaluation).

## Why Listers Underestimate Seasonal Risk

**They apply year-round commercial cap rates to seasonal businesses.**

Cap rates reflect risk. A property with 12-month stable income is lower-risk than one with three great months and nine tough ones. Lower-risk properties command lower cap rates. Higher-risk properties require higher cap rates to compensate investors for volatility.

Yet listers use the same 6–8% cap rates they'd use for an office building or retail plaza—properties with stable 12-month income.

**They assume peak-season income levels.**

Some listers value seasonal properties using peak-season revenue run rates. If winter is strong, they might project winter income rates across the full year, or heavily weight winter income in their annual calculation.

This is a fantasy number. January doesn't predict June.

**They don't account for labor and utility cost seasonality.**

A motel running 25% occupancy in May still needs to heat the building, staff a front desk (part-time at least), and maintain grounds. Utilities and labor costs don't drop proportionally to occupancy. Listers sometimes assume expenses drop with occupancy but don't—fixed costs remain.

**They anchor to speculative peak-market prices.**

Peak ski season sales of similar properties support high valuations. A 200-room hotel sold for $15M in 2021 during the pandemic peak gets used as a comparable for smaller properties. Listers extrapolate downward but anchor to the peak transaction.

Current market reality: fewer ski-season visitors, tighter margins, lower peak-season rates.

## The Geographic Challenge: Ski Resort Area "Spillover"

Properties within 5–10 miles of Killington, Okemo, Jay Peak, or other major ski resorts face additional overvaluation pressure.

Listers assume properties benefit from ski resort proximity and apply ski-market valuations to non-ski properties. A gas station, restaurant, or general store gets valued based on ski-season traffic potential, even if actual ski-related revenue is minimal.

**Example**: A small convenience store in Sherburne (base of Killington) was assessed at $320,000. Actual annual NOI was $28,000 (based on rent from a stable tenant). The lister assumed ski-season spillover traffic and applied a 8% cap rate ($28,000 ÷ 0.08 = $350,000), which rounds to the $320,000 assessment.

But the store doesn't see material ski traffic. Its business is stable, year-round, with no seasonal spike. A property with stable 12-month income should be valued at standard commercial cap rates (8–9%), not ski-season-influenced rates.

Fair value: $28,000 ÷ 0.085 = $329,000. The store was overvalued by the ski-season assumption.

## The Vacation Rental Crisis: Overvaluation by "Highest and Best Use"

Vacation rental properties—condos, houses, mixed-use buildings in ski or tourist towns—face systematic overvaluation based on highest-and-best-use doctrine.

A three-unit apartment building in a small town might be occupied by long-term residential tenants at $900/month each ($32,400 annual rent). Listers see it's in a desirable ski-area location and assume it could be converted to short-term vacation rental at $120–$150/night.

Fantasy calculation:
- 3 units at $135/night average (assuming 50% occupancy year-round, including shoulder seasons)
- Annual revenue: 3 × 135 × 365 × 0.50 = $74,000
- Operating expenses: $25,000
- NOI: $49,000
- Cap rate (8%): $612,500 valuation

Compared to actual current use:
- 3 units at $900/month = $32,400
- Operating expenses: $12,000
- NOI: $20,400
- Cap rate (8%): $255,000 valuation

Difference: $357,500 (139% overvaluation) based purely on speculative "highest and best use."

The property is owner-occupied or rented to long-term residential tenants. It's not a vacation rental. Yet listers value it as if it is.

**This is the most common overvaluation I see in ski and tourist areas.**

## Three Evidence Categories for Seasonal Property Appeals

When you file a grievance for seasonal property, bring evidence in three categories:

### 1. Actual Occupancy and Income Data

Bring actual occupancy records: guest registers, booking calendars, occupancy percentages by month.

Bring income documentation: rent rolls (if you're leasing units), P&L statements, tax returns showing actual annual revenue and NOI.

Bring rate data: document your actual average nightly rate by season. If winter averages $130/night and shoulder season averages $80, say so.

**Power of this evidence**: Listers can't argue with documented occupancy and income. If your building operated at 45% occupancy on average and they assumed 65%, you have a clear reduction case.

### 2. Comparable Sales with Seasonal Adjustments

Find recent sales of similar seasonal properties in Vermont or neighboring ski regions. Adjust for property type, size, location, and—critically—occupancy pattern.

Show that comparable seasonal hotels or rentals are trading at higher cap rates (9–12%) than year-round commercial property (7–8%), reflecting the added risk of seasonality.

**Example comparable analysis**:
- Comparable A: 20-unit motel near Okemo, sold 2024 for $900,000, NOI $85,000, implied cap rate 9.4%
- Comparable B: 15-unit motel near Killington, sold 2023 for $650,000, NOI $56,000, implied cap rate 8.6%
- Your property: Similar size, similar occupancy pattern, NOI $75,000, assessed at $1,050,000 (implies 7.1% cap rate)

Argument: Comparable seasonal properties trade at 8–9% cap rates, not 7%. Your property should be valued at 8–9% cap rate, not the 7% listers used.

Fair value: $75,000 ÷ 0.085 = $882,000 (vs. assessed $1,050,000)

### 3. Cost Approach as a Sanity Check

Calculate what it would cost to build your property new, apply depreciation for age and condition, and add land value.

For many aging seasonal properties (20+ years old), the cost approach produces a value significantly lower than the income approach. This floor value often supports a reduction case.

**Example**: A 25-year-old seasonal lodge assessed at $800,000 (income approach). Replacement cost: $600,000. Depreciation for age (25 years, 2% annual): ~60% depreciation = $240,000 remaining. Add land value ($150,000). Cost approach value: $390,000.

The huge gap between income approach ($800,000) and cost approach ($390,000) suggests income approach is too aggressive. Fair value is likely somewhere between them, probably $500,000–$600,000.

## Filing Your Grievance for Seasonal Property

When you file your grievance:

1. **Get documentation organized**: Tax returns showing actual NOI, occupancy records, guest registers
2. **Call your town lister** and confirm the grievance deadline (typically May 1)
3. **Prepare a clear one-page grievance letter** stating the assessed value is excessive and requesting a specific reduction with one key reason (seasonality, highest-and-best-use error, or occupancy assumptions)
4. **Gather 2–3 comparable seasonal property sales** with cap rate analysis
5. **Attend the grievance hearing** and present your evidence
6. **If denied, appeal to BCA within 14 days**
7. **For BCA hearing, bring:**
   - Detailed occupancy and income documentation
   - Comparable sales analysis
   - Cost approach calculation as sanity check
   - Photos of any deferred maintenance or condition issues

The BCA site visit is crucial for seasonal properties. The inspectors will see the actual property condition, compare it to the lister's assumptions, and often reduce valuations if they see the property doesn't support the assessed value.
