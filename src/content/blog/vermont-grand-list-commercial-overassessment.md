Vermont's Grand List system is theoretically sound. In practice, it creates systematic overvaluation of both residential and commercial properties. Understanding why this happens—and where the structural gaps are—is the key to winning your property tax grievance.

The problem isn't that listers are incompetent. It's that the system itself has fundamental gaps: outdated data, market timing lags, insufficient adjustment methodology, and property-specific blind spots that push assessments higher than fair market value. Once you understand these structural weaknesses, you can exploit them in your grievance with specific, defensible evidence.

## What Is the Grand List and How It Works

Vermont's Grand List is the official inventory of all taxable property in a town and its assessed values. The listers (or city assessor in larger municipalities) create and maintain the Grand List annually. The Grand List filed by April 1 determines the tax base for the entire town.

Every property gets a line on the Grand List: property ID, location, property type, assessed value, and tax classification. For residential property, this is "homestead" (primary residence, gets lower tax rate). For commercial property, rental property, and vacation homes, it's "nonhomestead" (higher tax rate).

Listers base their assessments on three valuation approaches:
- **Sales comparison approach**: Recent comparable sales, adjusted for differences
- **Income capitalization approach**: For properties generating income (rentals, commercial), dividing NOI by cap rate
- **Cost approach**: Replacement cost of the building minus depreciation, plus land value

In theory, this three-approach methodology is solid appraisal practice. In practice, several structural factors cause systematic overvaluation. Let me break down each one.

## Problem 1: The Data Lag and Market Volatility Gap

Here's the first critical weakness: **The Grand List is based on sales and data from 6–18 months before assessment.**

Listers assess property values in January and February for the April 1 Grand List. They're using sales data from the prior 12–18 months. In stable markets, this lag is manageable. But Vermont's market has experienced significant volatility:

- **2020–2021**: Prices surged 15–25% due to pandemic remote-worker migration into Vermont
- **2022–2023**: Market flattened and prices declined 8–15% as interest rates rose
- **2024–2026**: Market stabilized at lower levels than 2021–2022 peaks

Many listers use 2021–2022 comparable sales to value 2026 properties. They're anchored to peak-market data.

**For residential properties:**
A home that sold for $425,000 in early 2022 (peak market) might have sold for $375,000 in 2025. Yet listers often still use the 2022 sale as their primary comparable, adjusting for condition but not adequately for the market shift.

**Example**: Two nearly identical colonial homes on the same street. Home A sold in March 2022 for $420,000. Home B (your home) hasn't sold recently. Listers assess it at $410,000 using Home A's 2022 sale as primary comparable. But comparable sales in the past 6 months show similar homes in your area selling for $365,000–$380,000. The lister's 2022-anchored value is 8–12% too high.

**For commercial properties:**
A retail building that sold for $800,000 in early 2022 at peak market might now sell for $650,000–$700,000. Yet listers still use the $800,000 comparable as their anchor, adjusting only slightly for condition rather than accounting for the market decline.

**Commercial example**: A downtown office building comparable to one that sold for $750,000 in 2022. Listers assessed yours at $740,000. But comparable sales in the past 8 months show similar office buildings selling for $600,000–$630,000. The lister's value is 15–20% too high due to market lag.

### How This Affects Your Grievance

If your property's assessed value is anchored to 2021–2022 sales, you have strong evidence for overvaluation. Recent comparable sales (within the past 6–12 months) showing lower prices are your strongest evidence. You can argue: "The lister's assessment is based on sales from a peak market 3–4 years ago. Current market data shows similar properties selling 12–18% lower."

## Problem 2: The Cap Rate and Income Approach Trap

This is where commercial property and rental residential property overvaluation really happens.

When valuing income-producing property, appraisers use income capitalization:

**Fair Market Value = Net Operating Income ÷ Capitalization Rate**

Example: A rental property generates $60,000 annual NOI. If the cap rate is 6%, the property is worth $1,000,000. If the cap rate is 8%, it's worth $750,000. The cap rate choice determines value enormously.

Here's the structural problem: **Listers often use outdated or market-inflated cap rates that no longer reflect current investor expectations.**

During 2020–2022 (hot market), cap rates compressed (dropped to 5–7% for quality commercial property and lower for residential rentals). Listers adopted these compressed rates into their 2022–2023 assessments. But the market has shifted. Interest rates rose. Cap rates expanded back to 7–9% for commercial and 7–8% for residential rentals. Yet many listers still use 2021–2022 cap rates.

**Example - Commercial**: Small office building, $45,000 NOI. Lister assessment: $600,000 (using 7.5% cap rate). Current market data from recent sales: comparable office buildings are trading at 8.5–9% cap rates. Using 8.5% cap rate: Fair value = $45,000 ÷ 0.085 = $529,000. The lister's value is 13% too high purely because they used an outdated, compressed cap rate.

**Example - Residential Rental**: 4-unit rental building, $55,000 NOI. Lister assessment: $715,000 (using 7.7% cap rate from 2022). Current market: comparable 4-unit buildings are selling at 7.8–8.2% cap rates due to rising interest rates and investor caution. Using 8% cap rate: Fair value = $55,000 ÷ 0.08 = $687,500. The lister's value is 4% too high.

### How to Identify Cap Rate Overvaluation

If you own rental or income-producing property:

1. Calculate your actual annual NOI (gross rental income minus all operating expenses, not including your owner labor)
2. Research current market cap rates for your property type in your area:
   - Single-family rentals: typically 6.5–7.5% currently
   - Multifamily (2–4 units): 7–8%
   - Commercial office/retail: 7.5–9%
   - Industrial: 7–9%
3. Divide your NOI by a current market cap rate
4. Compare to your assessed value

If assessed value > (NOI ÷ current market cap rate), you're likely overvalued due to cap rate assumptions.

**Evidence for your grievance:**
- Recent comparable sales of similar income-producing properties
- Market cap rates from appraisals, commercial real estate databases, or recent comparable sales
- Documentation showing listers used outdated cap rates

## Problem 3: Occupancy and Lease-Term Assumptions

Listers often assume commercial and rental properties operate at peak occupancy with stable tenants. Reality is messier.

### Vacancy Overvaluation

A commercial or rental building with 25–40% vacancy (common in Vermont markets during economic slowdowns) isn't worth the same as a fully occupied comparable. Yet listers sometimes:
- Value assuming full occupancy, or
- Apply only a 3–5% vacancy factor when 15–30% is realistic

**Example**: An office building in your town has tenancy averaging 70% (reasonable for current market). Listers value it assuming 90% occupancy. Using realistic 70% occupancy rate would reduce the assessed value by about 20%.

### Tenant Quality and Stability

A property with a single tenant on a year-to-year lease (high risk) isn't worth the same as one with multiple long-term, credit-worthy tenants on multi-year leases (low risk). Yet listers sometimes don't adjust for:
- Single vs. multiple tenants
- Lease term length (1-year vs. 5-year+ leases)
- Tenant credit quality (national chain vs. local startup)

**Example**: A Chittenden County retail building with three tenants. Two have 1-year leases with unknown credit; one is a national chain on a 5-year lease. Listers value assuming high stability. Market-based value would reflect the actual lease-term and tenant-credit mix.

### In-Place Lease Rate Discounts

Rents increase over time. A building with leases signed in 2018–2019 likely has rents 8–15% below current market rates. Yet listers sometimes:
- Value based on current market rents, not in-place rents, or
- Fail to account for the rent growth buildup over time

**Example**: Your property has in-place rents of $18/sq ft (leases from 2019). Market rents are now $21/sq ft. Lister values based on market rents, not in-place rents. But fair market value for a property with below-market leases is lower than market-rent properties—a buyer would wait to re-lease space at lower rates.

### How to Fight Occupancy/Lease Issues

Evidence for your grievance:
- Actual occupancy documentation (lease list, rent roll, tenant list)
- Current market occupancy rates for comparable buildings
- Copy of your leases showing actual lease terms and in-place rents
- Market rent data showing in-place vs. market rate difference
- Documentation of tenant credit quality

## Problem 4: Highest-and-Best-Use Overvaluation

Some listers value commercial property based on "highest and best use"—what the property could theoretically be worth if redeveloped or converted—rather than actual use and actual income.

**Examples:**
- A property near a ski resort is valued assuming ski lodge income potential, even though it's actually a quiet residential rental generating modest rental income
- A mixed-use building in a town with hotel zoning is valued assuming hotel conversion is imminent, even though it operates successfully as retail/office with no conversion plans
- A farmland parcel is valued based on development potential, even though the owner operates it as a farm

This "speculative potential" overvaluation inflates assessed values 15–30% above what the property actually generates in income.

### Why This Is Wrong

Fair market value is what the property would sell for at market in its actual use, not its theoretical best use. A property generating $50,000 annual rental income isn't worth the same as a property generating $150,000 from a hotel, even if it's the same physical location.

Vermont law requires assessment based on fair market value in actual use. But some listers stretch this by applying the highest-and-best-use approach.

### How to Fight This

If you believe your property is overvalued due to highest-and-best-use assumptions:
- Argue that fair market value is based on actual use and actual income
- Document your actual use, actual income, and actual market conditions
- Provide comparable sales of properties in similar actual use (not potential use)
- Show that conversion to the "highest and best use" is not economically feasible or likely

**Example argument**: "The lister appears to value this property based on theoretical hotel conversion potential. However, conversion would require $200,000+ in capital expenditure and regulatory approval. My property is fairly valued based on its actual use as a residential rental generating $60,000 NOI annually."

## Problem 5: The Statewide Reappraisal and Equalization Lag

This is critical: **Towns that haven't been reappraised in 5+ years are operating on systematically stale baseline data.**

Vermont requires towns to conduct reappraisals periodically (roughly every 5–7 years, though timing varies). If your town's last reappraisal was 2019 or earlier, listers are working with:
- 2018–2019 comparable sales data
- Pre-pandemic market assumptions
- Property condition assessments that are 6+ years old
- Lister experience from a different market era

The market has shifted significantly. Comparables anchored to 2019 data are increasingly unreliable as 2026 approaches.

### Equalization Study Data

Vermont's Department of Taxes publishes an annual "Common Level of Appraisal" (CLA) study showing how accurately each town's assessments match actual market sales.

A CLA of:
- **95%**: Town is slightly undervalued (good for property owners)
- **105%**: Town is slightly overvalued (bad for property owners)
- **115%**: Town is 15% overvalued (very bad for property owners)

If your town has a high CLA (overvalued), this is strong evidence in your grievance: "Our town is systematically overvalued. The state's CLA study shows [town] assessments are 115% of market value. This supports the argument that my property is overvalued by approximately 15%."

| Town Reappraisal Status | Likely Overvaluation | CLA Impact | Evidence Strength |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reappraised 2024–2025 | 5–10% | Individual property variation | Moderate |
| Reappraised 2022–2023 | 10–18% | Some market shift since reappraisal | Strong |
| Reappraised 2020–2021 | 15–25% | 2021 peak anchoring + market decline | Very Strong |
| Not reappraised since 2019 | 20–35% | Stale data + multiple market cycles | Extremely Strong |

### How to Use Equalization Data

Find your town's CLA in the Vermont DOR equalization study (published annually). Use it in your grievance:

"The Department of Taxes' 2025 equalization study shows [town] properties are assessed at [CLA]% of market value. This indicates systematic overvaluation of [X]% across the town, which suggests individual properties, including mine, are likely overvalued."

## Problem 6: Inadequate Deferred Maintenance Adjustments

Listers are supposed to use cost approach valuation (replacement cost minus depreciation). In practice, many listers:
- Apply depreciation as a percentage rather than accounting for actual repair costs
- Don't adjust for known, documented deferred maintenance
- Miss functional obsolescence (outdated systems, poor layout)
- Miss external obsolescence (zoning changes, neighborhood decline)

**Example**: Two similar commercial office buildings. Building A (comparable): newer, well-maintained, sells for $500,000. Building B (yours): 1960s, original HVAC system, roof needing $65,000 replacement, aging electrical system. Lister values yours at $475,000 (applying a 5% depreciation factor for age). But fair value should account for the $65,000 required capital expenditure: roughly $435,000 ($500,000 − $65,000).

The lister misses this because they apply a generic depreciation percentage rather than adjusting for actual documented repair needs.

### How to Fight This

For your grievance:
- Get contractor estimates for any needed repairs or upgrades
- Document deferred maintenance with photos and specific descriptions
- Calculate cost approach adjustment: comparable value − actual repair costs needed = fair value
- Present evidence of functional obsolescence (poor layout, outdated systems, etc.)

## Problem 7: Rough Comparable Adjustments and Compounding Errors

Even when listers use comparables, their adjustment methodology can create significant errors.

**Example lister adjustment:**
- Comparable sold for $500,000
- Adjust +6% for larger square footage: $530,000
- Adjust −4% for worse location: $508,800
- Adjust +3% for newer construction: $524,064
- Subject property assessed value: $524,000

The problem: Small percentage adjustments compound. If each adjustment has a 5% accuracy error (quite possible when adjustments are rough estimates), the compounded error is 25%+. Lister final values are often well outside the reasonable range.

Moreover, listers often:
- Miss critical adjustments: tenant stability, lease rates, occupancy, actual condition
- Over-weight superficial ones: square footage, year built, lot size
- Apply the same percentage adjustment for different property types

### How to Challenge This

Present your own adjusted comparable analysis with:
- 5–8 strong comparables, not just 1–2
- Clear, specific dollar adjustments (not rough percentages)
- Adjustments for factors listers missed: occupancy, lease rates, tenant quality, condition
- Reconciliation of the different approaches to value (sales comparison, income, cost)

## How the System Creates Overvaluation: Complete Summary

Here's how these factors combine to create systematic overvaluation:

| Structural Gap | Mechanism | Typical Impact | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| Data lag (market volatility) | Using 18-month old sales in volatile market | 8–15% overvaluation | Recent comparable sales data |
| Cap rate anchoring | Using 2021–2022 compressed cap rates in 2025–2026 | 8–18% overvaluation | Current market cap rate research |
| Occupancy assumptions | Assuming peak occupancy, full tenant stability | 10–20% overvaluation | Actual occupancy/tenant documentation |
| Highest-and-best-use | Valuing speculative potential vs. actual use | 15–30% overvaluation | Document actual use and actual income |
| Stale reappraisal baseline | Using 5+ year old data and comparables | 20–35% overvaluation | Equalization study data, recent comps |
| Inadequate maintenance adjustments | Under-weighting repair and obsolescence costs | 5–15% overvaluation | Contractor estimates, cost approach |
| Rough adjustment methodology | Compounding small errors in comparables | 5–12% overvaluation | Precise adjustments with dollar amounts |

For most overassessed properties, **three or more of these factors apply simultaneously.** Combined, they regularly create 25–50% overvaluations.

## How to Use This Knowledge in Your Grievance

Now that you understand the structural gaps, your grievance strategy is clear:

1. **Identify which gaps apply to your property** (data lag, cap rate issue, occupancy assumption, highest-and-best-use valuation, stale reappraisal, deferred maintenance, adjustment errors?)

2. **Gather specific evidence for those gaps:**
   - Recent comparable sales (last 6–12 months)
   - Current market cap rates (if income property)
   - Actual occupancy documentation (if commercial/rental)
   - Equalization study data (is your town overvalued systematically?)
   - Contractor estimates for deferred maintenance
   - Documentation of actual use and actual income

3. **Present evidence showing the specific gaps lister missed**

4. **Propose a corrected value accounting for those gaps**

**Example residential grievance:**
"The assessed value of $410,000 appears based on a comparable sale from March 2022 during peak market prices. My analysis of comparable homes in this neighborhood that sold in 2025 shows prices of $365,000–$380,000. This represents a 6–12% market decline since the lister's comparable, which is consistent with statewide trends in 2022–2024. Adjusted for current market conditions, fair value is approximately $375,000."

**Example commercial grievance:**
"The assessed value of $620,000 appears to use a 7% capitalization rate applied to the property's NOI. However, current market cap rates for comparable office buildings in this area are 8.5–9%, reflecting current interest rate and market conditions. Additionally, my property operates at 72% occupancy, while the assessment appears to assume higher occupancy. Using current market cap rates (8.5%) and realistic occupancy (72%), fair market value is approximately $490,000."

## Wrapping It All Together

Vermont's Grand List system is legitimate. Listers are generally competent professionals. But the system has structural blind spots:
- Market timing lags
- Outdated cap rate assumptions
- Incomplete occupancy and lease adjustments
- Occasional highest-and-best-use inflation
- Stale reappraisal data in some towns

Knowing these gaps puts you in control. You can gather evidence-specific to your situation, present it persuasively, and significantly improve your chances of a successful grievance.
