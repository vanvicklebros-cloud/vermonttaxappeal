I get this question regularly from both residential homeowners and commercial property owners: "Can I just file a grievance myself and save the consultant fee?"

The answer is yes—Vermont doesn't legally require you to hire a professional. But should you? That depends on several critical factors: your property's assessed value, the magnitude of the overvaluation, your available time, your comfort with research and appraisal methodology, and your access to market data.

Let me break down the real economics, success rates, and decision framework for both residential and commercial properties.

## What a DIY Grievance Actually Requires: The Full Time Breakdown

If you choose to handle your appeal yourself, here's the complete work involved:

**Phase 1: Research and Market Analysis (10–30 hours)**

For residential homeowners:
- Locate your Grand List notice and property identification number (PIN)
- Research 5–8 comparable residential sales in your area (last 18–24 months)
- Obtain actual sale prices, dates, and property details (square footage, lot size, age, condition)
- Analyze how each comparable differs from your property (size adjustments, condition, location, amenities)
- Calculate a justified adjusted market value
- Document any property-specific issues: zoning restrictions, easements, functional problems, physical defects

For commercial property owners:
- Locate Grand List notice and PIN
- Research comparable commercial sales specific to your property type (retail, office, industrial, mixed-use, rental)
- Obtain detailed sale information and transaction documentation
- Create a comparable sales analysis with detailed adjustment justifications
- If income-producing property: research current market rental rates, occupancy rates, typical NOI multiples, capitalization rates
- If lease-encumbered: analyze lease terms, tenant credit quality, lease expiration dates
- Calculate replacement cost approach (construction cost minus depreciation) as a sanity check
- Research local market conditions affecting valuation: zoning changes, market shift, obsolescence

**Phase 2: Evidence Gathering (8–20 hours)**

For residential homeowners:
- Photograph interior and exterior clearly documenting condition
- Compile list of any needed repairs, maintenance items, functional obsolescence
- Obtain contractor estimates for major repairs (roof, HVAC, foundation, electrical, plumbing, etc.)
- Gather appraisals from purchase, refinance, or recent valuations
- Document any property restrictions: easements, covenants, zoning limitations, environmental issues
- Compile property history: tax records, prior assessments, previous grievances

For commercial property owners:
- Compile and organize all current lease agreements
- Gather 2–3 years of P&L statements, rent rolls, tenant lists
- Document actual occupancy, vacancy, tenant credit quality
- Photograph entire property documenting condition, deferred maintenance, functional obsolescence
- Obtain contractor estimates for deferred maintenance items
- Gather current appraisals or valuations if available
- Document any functional or economic obsolescence: structural limitations, incompatible use, market shift, regulatory issues

**Phase 3: Document Preparation (5–12 hours)**

- Write a clear, concise grievance letter explaining why the assessed value is wrong
- Prepare comparable sales analysis with exhibit-quality comparables and clear adjustment methodology
- Create a one-page executive summary showing your justified valuation vs. assessed value
- Prepare calculation worksheets or valuation models showing your work
- Organize all exhibits in logical presentation order (most important items first)
- Create a presentation outline or talking points for your hearing

**Phase 4: Hearing Preparation and Attendance (4–8 hours)**

- Prepare your verbal presentation (practice multiple times)
- Anticipate likely questions from listers and prepare answers
- Organize all documents so they're easy to reference and present during the hearing
- Prepare for property inspection (if BCA appeal follows)
- Attend the hearing (typically 1–2 hours of your time in the hearing room)

**Phase 5: BCA Appeal Preparation (if needed) (6–15 hours)**

If listers deny or partially deny your grievance:
- Prepare a more formal written appeal with legal citations
- Incorporate any new evidence not presented at lister hearing
- Address specific points listers made in their decision
- Prepare for BCA site inspection and hearing
- Attend BCA hearing and participate in site inspection

**Total Time Commitment: 35–85 hours**

For residential homeowners: roughly 40–50 hours (1–1.5 work weeks)
For commercial property owners: roughly 50–85 hours (1.5–2 work weeks)

For a commercial owner juggling business operations, this is substantial—equivalent to one full work week minimum, but often two weeks of part-time effort.

## The DIY Success Rate: What the Data Shows

Here's the honest data from Vermont property tax appeals: **DIY grievances have a 22–32% actual success rate** in producing meaningful assessment reductions.

When I say "success," I mean listers actually reduce the assessment by 5%+ (which is the minimum threshold for a meaningful tax savings). Many DIY grievances result in listers sustaining (upholding) the original valuation because the owner's evidence was incomplete, improperly analyzed, or presented ineffectively.

**Why DIY Grievances Fail (In Order of Frequency)**

**1. Weak or improperly adjusted comparables (45% of failures)**

DIY owners often find 1–3 comparable sales and present them without proper adjustment methodology. They might say, "House A sold for $375,000 and is similar to my house," without documenting property differences (square footage, lot size, condition, age, condition) and calculating specific dollar adjustments. Listers are trained appraisers who apply standard adjustment methodology. When they see unadjusted comparables, they reject them as unreliable.

For commercial properties: DIY owners find a warehouse sale or retail sale and try to use it as a comparable without adjusting for tenant quality, lease terms, location, or use differences. Commercial valuation is highly fact-specific. Improper comparables signal to listers that the owner doesn't understand commercial valuation methodology.

**2. No income approach analysis (35% of failures, primarily commercial)**

For rental residential property and income-producing commercial property, Vermont listers use three appraisal approaches: sales comparison, cost approach, and income approach. DIY owners frequently skip income analysis entirely, focusing only on comparable sales. Listers recognize this incomplete analysis and discount the grievance.

For a rental house generating $2,400/month: DIY owners may present comparable house sales but never analyze what a reasonable cap rate or income multiple suggests the property is worth. Listers know the income approach often produces a different (sometimes higher, sometimes lower) valuation than comparables alone. Skipping it signals incomplete analysis.

For commercial income property: Listers know your actual rental income, occupancy, and lease terms. A DIY owner who doesn't cite these figures or demonstrate market-based cap rates and NOI multiples appears to be arguing from emotion rather than data.

**3. Inadequate documentation of condition issues (30% of failures)**

Owners make unsupported claims: "The property has deferred maintenance" or "The roof is old and will need replacement soon." Listers ignore undocumented claims. If you claim $25,000 in deferred maintenance, provide contractor estimates. If you claim functional obsolescence, document what the specific limitation is and why it affects value.

Photos alone don't work. A photo showing an old roof is evidence that the roof is old, but it's not evidence that replacement is necessary or what the cost will be. Listers want contractor estimates, repair specifications, and condition documentation.

**4. Ineffective presentation and organization (25% of failures)**

Grievances that are long, emotional, or poorly organized receive less attention from listers than those that present clear data systematically. A five-page grievance letter that mixes comparable sales, personal anecdotes, and emotional arguments is less persuasive than a one-page summary with organized exhibits.

**5. Missing or misunderstanding equalization study data (20% of failures)**

Vermont's Department of Taxes publishes a "Common Level of Appraisal" (CLA) study for each town annually. This shows whether the town's assessments are systematically over- or under-valued relative to actual sales prices. If your town has a 95% CLA, it's undervalued. If it has a 115% CLA, it's overvalued by about 15%.

Many DIY owners don't know this data exists. When they do find it, they often misinterpret it. Using equalization data correctly can strengthen a grievance substantially. Missing it is a missed opportunity.

**6. Timing and deadline mistakes (15% of failures)**

Some DIY owners file the initial grievance but miscalculate the BCA appeal deadline (14 days from the lister's written decision, not 14 days from the hearing date). They miss it and lose their appeal right. Others don't attend their lister hearing and expect the written grievance to be persuasive (it rarely is).

**Aggregate Success Reality:**

Out of 100 DIY residential and commercial grievances filed in Vermont towns annually:
- Approximately 25–32 result in meaningful reductions (5%+)
- Approximately 40–50 result in no reduction (listers sustain original value)
- Approximately 18–30 result in minimal reductions (1–3%, which barely offset the inflation in property values)

The overall success rate is poor because listers are trained appraisers dealing with property owners who lack appraisal training, market knowledge, and comparative understanding of valuation methodology.

**Professional Consultant Success Rate:**

For comparison, when I handle grievances where the overvaluation is genuine and substantial (15%+):
- **Approximately 70–82% result in meaningful reductions (5%+)**
- Approximately 12–18% result in no reduction
- Approximately 8–12% result in minimal reductions

The success rate difference is 3x higher, which is not coincidental. It reflects experience, methodology, market knowledge, and persuasive presentation.

## Summary: DIY vs Professional Decision Framework

Use this quick decision tree:

**Is your overassessment less than $8,000?** → DIY (if you have spare time)

**Is your overassessment $8,000–$15,000?** → Depends on property type
- Straightforward residential, good evidence available → DIY possible
- Commercial, income-producing, or complex → Hire professional

**Is your overassessment more than $15,000?** → Hire professional (expected value heavily favors professional)

**Is your property income-producing?** → Hire professional (income approach analysis is critical and requires expertise)

**Did you already file DIY and get denied?** → Hire professional (you need fresh strategy and better evidence)

**Is this a specialized property type?** (vacation rental, ski resort, mixed-use, unique features) → Hire professional (market knowledge matters)

## The Bottom Line: Do the Math for Your Property

Both DIY and professional representation are legitimate paths. But the economics are clear:

**For properties with small overvaluations (<$8,000):** DIY may make sense if you have spare time and enjoy research. Don't expect a financial return on your effort, but you might recover some tax savings.

**For properties with substantial overvaluations ($12,000+):** Professional representation has 2–3x higher expected value than DIY because of the dramatically higher success rate (70%+ vs. 28%). The 30% contingency fee is a bargain compared to the probability of failure with DIY.

**For commercial or income-producing properties:** Professional representation is almost always justified because income approach analysis requires expertise most DIY owners lack.

**For properties that have been denied before:** Professional representation is your only path to overcoming the prior denial and finding new evidence.

The math isn't close. When you calculate expected value properly (success rate × tax savings − your costs), professional representation wins for most cases with genuine overvaluation.
